The Career Arc of Professor Leigh A. Burgoyne AM

Emeritus Professor, Flinders University of South Australia
From Chromosomes to Crime Scenes — Six Decades of Discovery (1966–present)

This page traces the scientific life of Leigh A. Burgoyne (born 22 September 1939 in Adelaide, South Australia), biochemist, inventor, and Emeritus Professor at Flinders University of South Australia. From his PhD in Adelaide in the 1960s to his most recent publication in 2025 at the age of 84, his career has spanned six decades and touched fields as diverse as enzyme chemistry, the fundamental structure of chromosomes, forensic science, and fungal genomics. He continues to research today.

Two achievements stand out. In 1973, working with his PhD student Dean Hewish, Leigh made a discovery that helped reveal how DNA is packaged inside every living cell. The work was so significant that Francis Crick described it as a “breakthrough” on BBC Radio, and that Roger Kornberg cited it as foundational evidence when developing the nucleosome model that earned Kornberg the 2006 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Two decades later, he invented the FTA card, a small piece of chemically treated paper that revolutionised how DNA samples are collected and stored worldwide, from crime scenes to newborn screening programmes to wildlife conservation in the field.

This page is both a scientific record and a family tribute. The career arc below tells the story of how each phase of research has built on what came before. Every publication is linked to a detailed summary. Whether you are a molecular biologist or simply someone who wants to understand what Leigh has devoted his life to, we hope this page does justice to a remarkable and ongoing career.

“I see myself as a chromatin biochemist. I’ve always been interested in how DNA is packaged and what happens when you disturb that packaging — whether you’re trying to copy it, read it, store it, or watch a cell die.”

Career Arc

Phase 1: Yeast Enzymology & Early Biochemistry (1966–1969)

▼

In his twenties, Leigh trained as a biochemist, first at the University of Adelaide, then at Yale. He learned how to take biological molecules apart and figure out how they work: skills that would underpin everything he did for the next fifty years.

Read more ▼

Leigh Burgoyne's scientific career began at the University of Adelaide, where he earned a BSc in Agricultural Science (1962), followed by Honours in Agricultural Biochemistry (1963). He then undertook his PhD in Biochemistry under the supervision of Robert H. Symons, one of Australia's foremost biochemists, graduating in May 1968. The doctoral work centred on the enzyme L-lactate dehydrogenase (cytochrome b2) from baker's yeast — a flavoprotein that raised questions about protein-nucleic acid interactions and enzyme structure.

Key outputs (1966–1967): Four publications on cytochrome b2, including crystallisation methods (published in Methods in Enzymology), the DNA component of the enzyme, and its molecular structure as revealed by electron microscopy with co-author Pamela Y. Dyer. An early paper with R. G. Nicholls and M. R. Atkinson laid methodological groundwork.

After completing his PhD, Burgoyne crossed the Pacific as a Fulbright Scholar for a postdoctoral fellowship at Yale University with Norman H. Giles, a distinguished fungal geneticist. Here the focus shifted to the arom multienzyme complex of Neurospora crassa, a large molecular machine in a bread mould that carries out five chemical reactions in a row.

Key outputs (1969): Two papers with Mary E. Case and Giles: one on in vivo/in vitro complementation between DHQ synthetase mutants (Genetics, 65 citations), and a BBA paper on purification and properties of the arom aggregate (100 citations), the most-cited paper of Phase 1.
Legacy: These six papers established Burgoyne as a skilled biochemist comfortable with protein purification, enzyme kinetics, electron microscopy, and fungal genetics. The arom work was widely cited in the multienzyme complex field for decades. The Adelaide training with Symons and Atkinson would prove pivotal for his next move.

Phase 2: DNA Synthesis & Replication (1970–1974)

▼

Back in Australia at the newly founded Flinders University, Leigh made the discovery of his career: the first evidence that DNA inside our cells is not just a loose thread, but is wound around tiny protein spools in a regular, repeating pattern. This structure, later named the “nucleosome,” is fundamental to how every plant and animal packages and controls its genetic information. The discovery was cited by Roger Kornberg as key evidence for his nucleosome model, work for which Kornberg received the 2006 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

Read more ▼

Returning to Australia, Burgoyne joined Flinders University, a young institution that had opened just a few years earlier, and launched a programme on DNA replication in mammalian cells. He worked closely with M. Anwar Waqar and Maurice R. Atkinson, his former Adelaide colleague.

Personal note: M. R. Atkinson died on 30 May 1970, a significant loss. The early papers of this period were written with Atkinson as co-author; his influence shaped the calcium-dependent endonuclease programme that would culminate in the landmark 1973 discovery.

The initial focus was on calcium-dependent priming of DNA synthesis in isolated nuclei and the use of permealysed cell systems (cells made permeable to small molecules while keeping their internal replication machinery intact). This technically demanding approach allowed Burgoyne to study how cells copy their DNA under near-natural conditions, observing the short intermediate fragments (Okazaki fragments) produced during copying and the effects of chain-terminating drug molecules like ara-CTP.

Three papers with Waqar (1970–1971) and a sole-author paper on permealysed Ehrlich ascites cells (1972) built toward a fundamental question: what is the enzyme that produces the regular DNA fragments observed in nuclear preparations?

The Landmark: Hewish & Burgoyne, 1973. Together with PhD student Dean R. Hewish, Burgoyne demonstrated that a calcium/magnesium-dependent nuclear endonuclease digests chromatin DNA at regularly spaced sites, producing a "ladder" of fragments in multiples of approximately 200 base pairs. This was the first biochemical evidence for the repeating subunit structure of chromatin, what would soon be named the nucleosome. In simple terms: they showed that DNA is not randomly stuffed into the cell nucleus, but is wrapped around protein spools at precise, regular intervals, like thread wound around a string of beads.
Impact (~1,172 citations): The 1973 BBRC paper was directly cited by Roger Kornberg in his foundational 1974 paper proposing the nucleosome model, and again in Kornberg's 2006 Nobel Prize lecture. A citation count of over 1,100 places this among the most influential papers in the history of chromatin biology: more than a thousand subsequent scientific papers have built on this work. A companion paper with Hewish on the calcium-dependent endonuclease itself garnered an additional 126 citations. In 1982, the paper was designated a Citation Classic by Current Contents, one of only ten Australian biomedical papers ever to receive that distinction.
Study leave — MRC Mill Hill, London (1973–74, 9 months): Following the landmark discovery, Burgoyne spent nine months at the Medical Research Council's National Institute for Medical Research at Mill Hill, London, working in J. Tata's laboratory. Mill Hill was one of the world's premier biomedical research institutes; its scientists had already won multiple Nobel Prizes. This placement gave Burgoyne direct access to the leading edge of chromatin and gene regulation research at a pivotal moment in the field.

The phase closed with the 1974 paper by Burgoyne, Hewish, and Jennifer Mobbs extending the chromatin substructure work using two-dimensional polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (142 citations), and a pharmacological paper on ara-CTP's effects in permealysed cells (22 citations).

Phase 3: Chromatin Structure & Higher-Order Organisation (1975–1992)

▼

Having shown that DNA is wound around protein spools, Leigh spent nearly twenty years asking the next question: how are those spools themselves arranged? He also opened a second major line of research into “satellite DNA,” mysterious repetitive sequences scattered through our chromosomes, which would eventually lead him toward forensic science and species identification.

Read more ▼

Building on the nucleosome discovery, Burgoyne spent nearly two decades probing the higher-order organisation of chromatin. This was one of the great open questions of molecular biology: how are nucleosomes arranged in three dimensions?

The dinucleosome concept and the fold-back model: With long-term collaborator Jennifer D. Skinner, Burgoyne developed the idea that chromatin has an alternating two-nucleosome (dinucleosomal) repeat — a "zigzag" superstructure rather than the coiled-spring (solenoid) models favoured by others. In a sole-author 1985 paper in Cytobios, Burgoyne formally proposed his alternative: a back-to-back fold-back model in which the zig-zag nucleofilament doubles back on itself in hairpin loops, with no coiling at all. This geometry naturally explains the alternating pattern (every second nucleosome shielded by its back-to-back partner), predicts that DNA base sequences control higher-order folding, and even suggests a primitive mechanism for chromosomes to find their matching partner during cell division. The paper was published in a small journal and went largely unnoticed. Leigh believes this contributed to incorrect coiled depictions of chromatin persisting in textbooks and on Wikipedia for decades. Subsequent experimental papers (1987) further eliminated coiling models, and this zigzag architecture was eventually confirmed by advanced microscopy techniques.

Key collaborators included Alistair J. Marshall (chromatin replication and nuclear body studies, 1976–1977), Graeme T. P. Saccone (trypsin resistance of chromatin superstructure, 1983), and Steven J. Davis (coherent disomal series, 1987).

Study leave — MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology, Cambridge (1977–78, 8 months): Burgoyne spent eight months at the MRC LMB in Cambridge, one of the most celebrated molecular biology laboratories in the world and home to multiple Nobel laureates. This placement deepened his understanding of chromatin higher-order structure during a critical period when the field was debating how nucleosomes fold into the 30 nm fibre.

In parallel, a major programme on satellite DNA emerged through collaboration with J.C.S. Fowler, Roger D. Drinkwater, and Skinner. Satellite DNA consists of short sequences repeated thousands or millions of times in a row along the chromosome. This group characterised human satellite III DNA, identified a five-letter repeating unit (TTCCA), coined the term "macrosatellite" for very large blocks of such repeats, and explored what these repetitive sequences could reveal about primate evolution and forensic identification.

Study leave — Erasmus University Rotterdam, D. Bootsma group (1981–82, 6 months): Burgoyne spent six months in the laboratory of Dirk Bootsma, a leading human geneticist specialising in DNA repair disorders. This stay in Rotterdam directly informed the subsequent collaboration with Peter McKinnon on ataxia telangiectasia, a DNA repair disease that Bootsma's group had helped characterise at the cellular level.
Ataxia telangiectasia studies: With Peter J. McKinnon (1984–1985), Burgoyne investigated cellular abnormalities in patients with ataxia telangiectasia, a rare inherited disease affecting DNA repair that causes progressive neurological disability. They found abnormalities in a structural protein called actin in cells from affected patients. McKinnon went on to become a leading researcher in this field.
Study leave — Edinburgh University, D. Finnegan group (1991, 5 months): Burgoyne spent five months in the laboratory of David Finnegan, a pioneer of transposable element research. Finnegan's group at Edinburgh was at the forefront of understanding how mobile DNA elements move around within genomes, work that connected directly to Burgoyne's interest in the repetitive sequences and satellite DNA that the laboratory's avian genome programme was uncovering.

Avian genome studies with Xiaoyi Zhu (1990–1992) extended the satellite DNA work to bird genomes, characterising macrosatellites in the Toulouse goose and repetitive sequences conserved across water birds. This species-level work anticipated the later wildlife identification programme.

Cell signalling: With Alessandro Testori and Andrew W. Murray, Burgoyne explored how cells send internal chemical signals to modify chromatin structure (1988–1992), showing that the three-dimensional shape of chromatin proteins matters for how they receive those signals.
Early forensic DNA engagement (1990): Burgoyne co-edited one of the very first books on the use of DNA in forensic science, DNA In Forensic Science: Theory, Techniques And Applications (Ellis Horwood, with J. Robertson and A.M. Ross), and wrote Chapter 2. This predated the FTA card patent by four years and marks the earliest published evidence of his transition toward forensic applications.
Legacy: Phase 3 produced over two dozen papers across multiple themes, establishing Burgoyne as an authority on chromatin higher-order structure, satellite DNA biology, and the molecular biology of repetitive sequences. The dinucleosome/zigzag model anticipated structural insights confirmed decades later by cryo-EM studies.

Phase 4: DNA Forensics, FTA Technology & Applied Biology (1993–present)

▼

In his fifties, Leigh reinvented himself. He turned his deep knowledge of how DNA behaves (how it degrades, how it can be protected, how it can be read) into practical tools. His most important invention was a small piece of chemically treated paper (the FTA card) that lets anyone, anywhere in the world, collect a DNA sample simply by pressing it onto the card. No refrigeration, no laboratory, no special training needed. This one invention transformed forensic science, newborn health screening, and wildlife conservation. His most recent publication, in 2025 at the age of 84, cites his own fifty-year-old discovery, and he is still going.

Read more ▼

The longest and most diverse phase of Burgoyne's career marked a decisive turn toward applied molecular biology, anchored by a transformative invention.

The FTA Card (1994): Burgoyne's foundational patent (US 5,496,562) described a chemically treated cellulose-based solid medium for storing, purifying, and processing DNA from biological samples at room temperature. The technology, incorporating chemicals that break open cells, protect DNA from enzymes that would destroy it, and neutralise damaging free radicals, became the FTA card after licensing to Whatman (later acquired by GE Healthcare, now part of Cytiva). A drop of blood, a cheek swab, or a leaf sample pressed onto the card is preserved indefinitely at room temperature and can be analysed years later.
Real-world impact: FTA cards became a global standard for DNA sample collection. They are used by police forces worldwide to collect evidence from crime scenes, by hospitals to screen newborn babies for genetic diseases, by veterinarians and conservationists to identify and track wildlife species, and by researchers working in remote locations where refrigeration is impossible. Burgoyne received approximately 19 patents spanning the core technology, swab devices, glass-fiber variants, RNA storage, automated processing, and photolytic release mechanisms.

With Craig D.G. Rogers, Burgoyne demonstrated FTA's utility for bacterial typing without DNA extraction (1997, 85 citations) and for reverse transcription of RNA from FTA paper (2000). The wildlife application, developed with Lisa M. Smith, yielded a highly cited BMC Ecology paper (2004, 272 citations) on field collection and processing of wildlife DNA using FTA.

Pyknosis — Leigh's proudest paper (1999): Of all his publications, Leigh says the sole-author paper on pyknosis (Experimental Cell Research, 65 citations) is the one he is most proud of. It asked a deceptively simple question: when a cell dies, its nucleus visibly shrinks and darkens (a process called pyknosis, observed under the microscope for over a century), but what actually happens to the DNA inside? Burgoyne showed that the DNA, still wound around its protein spools (the nucleosomes he helped discover in 1973), undergoes a conformational contraction, like thread pulling tight on its beads, driven by the sharing of histone tails between neighbouring spools. The paper drew together threads from every phase of his career: the calcium-dependent nuclease (1970s), the avian erythrocyte chromatin superstructure (1980s), the conformational sensitivity work (1992), and the cell lysis observations from FTA development (1997). It remains one of the most integrative conceptual papers in the chromatin literature, still attracting citations in the 2020s.
Forensic DNA programme: From 2003 onward, Burgoyne contributed to forensic casework methodology, developing DNA profiling tools used in criminal investigations, including methods for sexual assault cases (with Chang Yuet Meng and Katrin Both) and studies of genetic markers used for sex determination in forensic testing (2007, 71 citations). A wire-based rapid DNA sampling method was developed with Tong Chen and K. Paul Kirkbride for identifying victims of disasters.
Soil DNA forensics: Leigh pioneered a method for "fingerprinting" soil by reading the DNA of the thousands of microorganisms living in it. Every handful of soil contains a unique community of bacteria and fungi, like a biological postcode. His SMIPS patent (2004) and subsequent programme with David E.A. Catcheside, Anastasia S. Khodakova, Lin Y. Koh, Damien Abarno, and Adrian Linacre (2012–2017) developed this into a forensic tool that could link a suspect's shoes or a vehicle's tyres to a specific location. A PLoS ONE paper (2014, 49 citations) was the programme's most-cited output. Drug bioprofiling with the Australian Federal Police was explored with Catcheside and Kirkbride (NDLERF Monograph, 2008).

With Linden H. Seah, Burgoyne studied how light damages DNA on dried samples (2001, 2003), providing models for how stored DNA ages over time, important for understanding the long-term reliability of FTA cards and other archives.

Most recent paper (2025): At approximately 84 years of age, Burgoyne published in the Journal of Fungi on methodology for extracting high-quality DNA from wild mushrooms and other fungi collected in the Australian bush, co-authored with David E.A. Catcheside, Pamela S. Catcheside, and an international team. The method works by keeping DNA protected inside its natural protein packaging (chromatin) during the early purification steps — the same chromatin biology Leigh discovered in 1973. This paper cites his own landmark discovery from more than fifty years earlier. A career come full circle, and still continuing.

Honours & Awards

Over the course of his career, Leigh's work has been recognised by scientific organisations, industry bodies, and the South Australian government. These awards span the full range of his contributions, from fundamental discovery to technological innovation.

1982
Citation Classic — Current Contents
The 1973 Hewish & Burgoyne chromatin substructure paper was designated a Citation Classic, a recognition given to the most frequently cited papers in the scientific literature. It was one of only ten Australian biomedical papers ever to receive this distinction.
1999
Australian Technology Award — Finalist
Nominated in May 1999 for the FTA card technology, which was recognised as one of Australia's most significant recent technological innovations.
1999
SA Great Award for Science
Awarded in December 1999 by the SA Great organisation, recognising South Australians who have achieved national or international distinction. Leigh received the award for his contributions to science, particularly the invention of FTA technology.
2006
Outstanding Contribution to Flinders University
Recognised by Flinders University for his exceptional service to the institution across four decades of teaching, research, and innovation.
2012
Two NIFS Awards — Best Paper & Best Technical Article
The National Institute of Forensic Science recognised Leigh's forensic DNA work with two awards in the same year: best paper and best technical article, reflecting his dual contributions to both the science and the practical methodology of forensic DNA analysis.
2024
Member of the Order of Australia (AM)
Appointed in the 2024 Australia Day Honours for significant service to science, particularly through the development of DNA technologies. Invested by Her Excellency the Honourable Frances Adamson AC, Governor of South Australia, at Government House Adelaide. Characteristically, Leigh was most struck not by his own recognition but by the bravery citations awarded to others at the ceremony: “You don’t plan to do good; you’ve just got to be ready to do it.”
