A More Sensitive Method for the Quantitation of Genomic DNA by Alu Amplification

Authors: Melanie E. Sifis, Katrin Both, Leigh A. Burgoyne

Context & Background

By 2002, forensic DNA profiling had become dramatically more sensitive. STR amplification kits like Profiler-Plus could generate full profiles from just a few picograms of template DNA — far below the 150 pg detection limit of standard slot-blot quantitation methods used in forensic laboratories. This created a troubling legal problem: caseworkers were routinely generating valid DNA profiles from samples that registered as "zero DNA" on their quantitation assays. In court, defence counsel challenged such evidence, arguing that if the quantitation showed no DNA, the profile must be contamination, false alleles, or artefact.

This paper, developed at the South Australia Forensic Science Centre with Burgoyne's collaboration, solved the problem by exploiting the million-copy abundance of Alu repeats in the human genome. Alu is a 282-nucleotide primate-specific short interspersed element (SINE) that constitutes 5–10% of human DNA and occurs roughly one million times per haploid genome. By amplifying a conserved 229 bp core region of Alu with fluorescently labelled primers, the method achieved 100-fold greater sensitivity (2.5–100 pg detection range) than blot-based quantitation, bringing DNA measurement into alignment with profiling sensitivity.

The work built directly on the forensic DNA infrastructure Burgoyne had helped establish through FTA technology in the 1990s. First author Melanie Sifis was a student working with senior forensic scientist Dr Katrin Both at the SA Forensic Science Centre, with Burgoyne providing conceptual guidance and support through Flinders University's Biotechnology Program.

Key Findings

  • 100-fold increase in quantitation sensitivity: The Alu-based PCR method achieved a detection range of 2.5–100 pg DNA, compared to the 150 pg–10 ng range of slot-blot procedures. This brought quantitation into alignment with the sensitivity of STR profiling kits like Profiler-Plus.
  • Primate-specific detection: Because Alu repeats are primate-specific, the method avoids false-positive quantitation from bacterial, fungal, plant, or non-primate animal DNA contamination — a critical requirement for forensic casework involving degraded or contaminated samples.
  • Fluorescent primer design: A FAM-6 labelled primer pair (SP1/SP2) amplifying a 229 bp core sequence from within the 282 bp Alu consensus enabled detection on the ABI PRISM 377 Genetic Analyser. Quantitation was achieved by comparing fluorescence intensity (RFU, relative fluorescent units) to a standard curve run in duplicate alongside unknowns.
  • Optimized cycling conditions: 40 PCR cycles provided maximum sensitivity without excessive non-specific amplification. Fewer cycles reduced sensitivity; more cycles increased variability from non-specific products. The method used standard forensic PCR reagents (TaqGold, PCR Buffer II, 1 mM MgCl2).
  • Linear standard curve with R2 = 0.9605: Template DNA amounts (2.5–100 pg) plotted against 229 bp product peak height (RFU) yielded a highly linear relationship, allowing reliable back-calculation of unknown DNA concentrations. Standard deviations were generally small (3–113 RFU), though occasional stochastic variation occurred at very low template amounts.
  • Proof-of-concept on trace forensic samples: The method successfully quantitated DNA from gloves (51 pg), knives (27 pg), cups (13 pg), computer mice (67 pg), and elevator buttons (11 pg) — all samples that would register as "zero DNA" with slot-blot methods but could yield STR profiles.
  • Compatible with forensic workflow: Total assay time (6 h) was only double that of slot-blot procedures (3 h), and the method used equipment already present in forensic labs (thermal cyclers, genetic analysers). The slight time trade-off was justified for critically important low-level samples.
  • Inhibitor detection by dilution: The high sensitivity allowed ~100-fold dilution of samples while still expecting an Alu signal if sufficient DNA was present. This enabled detection of PCR inhibitors (hemoglobin, urea, heparin, glove powder, bacterial contaminants) and provided proof that useable DNA existed before embarking on purification procedures.

Significance & Contribution

Forensic impact: This paper addressed a major courtroom challenge to forensic DNA evidence in the early 2000s. By providing quantitation sensitivity matched to profiling sensitivity, it allowed forensic scientists to demonstrate that trace DNA samples contained DNA at levels sufficient for profiling, countering defence arguments about contamination or artefact. The method became part of the toolkit for handling low-level DNA samples in forensic casework.

Technical innovation: The use of Alu repeats for quantitation was conceptually elegant — the million-copy abundance provided built-in signal amplification, and the primate specificity ensured accurate measurement of human DNA even in heavily contaminated samples. The fluorescent detection approach anticipated the move toward real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR) methods that would become standard in the following years.

Career context: This paper exemplifies Burgoyne's Phase 4 applied forensic biology work. Published three years after the 1999 pyknosis paper (his proudest sole-author achievement) and eight years after the 1994 FTA patent, it shows Burgoyne continuing to mentor forensic science practitioners and apply molecular biology to practical casework problems. The collaboration with the SA Forensic Science Centre reflects his ongoing role as a resource for the forensic community.

Citation influence: With 36 citations, the paper has had moderate but sustained impact. It contributed to the development of human-specific DNA quantitation methods and influenced the adoption of qPCR-based quantitation in forensic laboratories worldwide (e.g., Quantifiler kits using multi-copy nuclear targets).

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Houck, Rinehart & Schmid (1979) J. Mol. Biol.: Discovery of Alu as a ubiquitous repetitive DNA family in the human genome — named for the AluI restriction site
  • Deininger & Batzer (1999) Mol. Genet. Metab.: Alu repeats and human disease — established primate-specificity and copy number (~1 million per haploid genome)
  • Schmid (1998) Nucleic Acids Res.: SINE evolution and Alu function — characterized the 282 bp consensus sequence and poly(A) tail
  • Waye et al. (1989) Biotechniques: Simple and sensitive method for quantifying human genomic DNA in forensic specimen extracts — established the slot-blot standard (150 pg–10 ng range) that this paper sought to improve upon
  • Martin, Butler & Bronstein (1995) Biotechniques: Quantitation of PCR products with chemiluminescence — precedent for using signal amplification for sensitive DNA measurement
  • Wilson (1997) Appl. Environ. Microbiol.: Inhibition and facilitation of nucleic acid amplification — catalogued common PCR inhibitors in forensic and environmental samples
  • Burgoyne's FTA technology (1994 patent onward): Established infrastructure for DNA sample handling, storage, and extraction in forensic casework — provided the context for this quantitation development

What It Contributed To

  • First demonstration of Alu-based quantitation for forensic DNA at picogram sensitivity (2.5–100 pg range)
  • Solved the legal/courtroom problem of "zero DNA" quantitation from profile-generating samples
  • Anticipated the move toward multi-copy target quantitation in forensic laboratories (later implemented via real-time qPCR platforms like Quantifiler)
  • Provided primate-specific quantitation resistant to bacterial, fungal, plant, and non-primate animal contamination
  • Demonstrated utility of Alu repeats as forensic targets (sensitivity, specificity, compatibility with existing lab workflows)
  • Established dilution-based approach for detecting PCR inhibitors in low-level samples
  • Contributed to forensic best-practice guidelines for handling trace DNA evidence
  • Influenced development of commercial qPCR quantitation kits targeting multi-copy nuclear sequences
