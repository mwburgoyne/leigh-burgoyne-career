FTA Technology DNA Storage RNA Preservation Glass Fiber Matrix Surfactant Protection Divisional Patent

Context & Background

This 2005 patent application publication (US 2005/0282180 A1) represents a divisional filing from the FTA patent family. Filed in December 2004 as a division of US Ser. No. 09/724,060, the application traces its lineage through the entire chain of continuations back to the original 1988 Australian priority filing. By 2005, FTA cards had become a global standard in forensic science, newborn screening, and field biology. While the parent specification remained essentially unchanged, this divisional carved out distinctly new claim territory reflecting the technology’s evolution.

Key Claims & Innovations

  • Generalised “genetic material” language: Unlike earlier patents that focused on DNA (particularly blood DNA), this application’s independent claims use the broader term “genetic material,” encompassing both DNA and RNA. Dedicated RNA preservation claims (Claims 11, 16, 19–23) represent entirely new scope not present in predecessor patents.
  • Glass fiber matrix claims: Explicitly claims fiber matrices comprising “synthetic glass fibers and filaments” (Claims 8, 14, 17–18), a significant departure from the cellulose-based papers of earlier FTA patents. Encompasses Whatman GF/A through GF/F grades.
  • Simplified surfactant-centric claims: Independent claims require only a fiber matrix and a surfactant — without mandating the full Tris/EDTA/SDS/urate cocktail. The chelating agent, weak base, and uric acid are relegated to dependent claims, maximising defensive scope.
  • “Releasing means” claim: Claim 9 introduces a “releasing means for releasing the genetic material from said fiber matrix,” anticipating integrated elution mechanisms in next-generation devices.

Significance & Contribution

This divisional represents a strategic IP move by Burgoyne and Whatman, Inc. to extend the FTA patent family’s coverage into RNA preservation and glass fiber substrates — two domains that had become commercially significant by the mid-2000s. The expansion to RNA was particularly forward-looking, as viral RNA detection, gene expression studies, and RNA-based diagnostics created demand for room-temperature RNA storage solutions. By grounding these new claims in the original 1988 priority date, the filing created formidable prior art coverage. The glass fiber claims reflected the maturation of FTA technology from simple cellulose filter paper into engineered substrates optimised for specific analytical workflows.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • US 5,496,562 (1996): Foundational FTA patent with Tris/EDTA/SDS/uric acid chemistry
  • US 5,807,527, US 5,972,386, US 5,985,327: Continuation patents refining core composition
  • US 6,294,203, US 6,322,983, US 6,447,804, US 6,627,226: Later continuations expanding claims
  • US 2004/0014068 A1: CIP introducing synthetic glass fiber matrices

What It Contributed To

  • Extended FTA patent protection to RNA storage for viral diagnostics and gene expression research
  • Secured IP coverage for glass fiber FTA products (e.g., FTA Elute cards) with improved release characteristics
  • Provided Whatman with broad defensive claims using simplified surfactant-only formulations
  • Supported commercial expansion into automated laboratory systems requiring engineered fiber substrates
