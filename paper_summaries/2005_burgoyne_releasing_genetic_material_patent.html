Context & Background

By the mid-2000s, Burgoyne’s FTA technology had become the standard method for collecting and storing genetic material on solid media. However, a critical limitation persisted: high molecular weight genomic DNA does not release well from any medium. Human DNA consists of enormously long molecules that must be fragmented to a controlled, optimal size for reliable downstream applications such as DNA microarrays and multiple-well plate analytical systems used in SNP surveys. Existing methods for breaking DNA in coloured lysates — intense sound waves, mechanical methods, or chemical approaches — were each plagued by problems of safety, automation difficulty, contamination, or unreliability. This patent addressed that gap by introducing a photolysis-based method for controllably releasing DNA from FTA and other solid media after washing away interfering pigments.

Key Claims & Innovations

  • Photolytic release method: A wash solution containing a buffer and a photolytic agent (preferably methylene blue) is applied to genetic-material-bearing solid media under dark or subdued light conditions. Subsequent controlled exposure to a light source releases DNA in fragments of defined, controllable size.
  • Controllable fragment size: Light exposure duration and photolytic agent concentration can be precisely tuned (down to microsecond intervals with a laser) to produce DNA fragments of optimal length for downstream applications, typically above 10 kilobases.
  • SDS as critical co-releasing agent: Sodium dodecyl sulfate in the wash solution was essential for efficient photolytic release; without SDS, even high concentrations of methylene blue with extended light exposure produced minimal DNA release.
  • Practical and safe: Methylene blue (NIH National Toxicology Program safety-assessed) with a conventional 8-watt fluorescent lamp and 10 minutes exposure sufficed for substantial DNA release.
  • Devices and kits: 51 claims covering devices (solid medium, light source, light-blocking cover) and kits for practicing the methods.
  • Broad applicability: Applies to FTA-coated materials, filter paper, silica with chaotropic agents, DNA-processing media with low positive charge, and any solid medium that binds genetic material.

Significance & Contribution

This patent solved the persistent problem of controllable DNA release from solid media. While Burgoyne’s earlier FTA patents established methods for capturing, purifying, and storing DNA, this invention completed the workflow by providing a reliable, automatable, and safe method for releasing DNA in fragments of defined size. The photolytic approach was particularly well suited to robotic environments and high-throughput applications, as light exposure is simply programmable. This was essential for the emerging era of DNA microarrays and large-scale SNP genotyping, where controlled fragment sizes and high DNA concentrations were required for process repeatability. The patent extended the commercial utility of the Whatman FTA product line from a sample collection and storage platform to a complete sample-to-analysis pipeline. The methylene blue photolysis concept builds directly on the Seah & Burgoyne (2001, 2003) work on photosensitizer-initiated attacks on dry DNA.
