Resistance of Chromatin Superstructure to Tryptic Digestion Modulated by Conjugated Polyacrylamide

Authors: G. T. P. Saccone, J. D. Skinner and L. A. Burgoyne*

Journal: FEBS Letters, 157(1), 111–114

Year: 1983

Received: 25 March 1983

DOI: —

Citations: ~15–30 (estimated)

Context & Background

Having used DNase-I-armed probes (attached to ferritin and BSA) to map nucleosome accessibility in chromatin (1981, 1982), the Burgoyne lab now extends the size-selective probe concept to proteases. Instead of asking which DNA sites are accessible, this paper asks which histone proteins are exposed at different levels of chromatin organisation. Free trypsin (4.2 nm diameter) serves as the small probe, while trypsin conjugated to soluble polyacrylamide (PA-trypsin, ~10–30 nm diameter) serves as the large probe. By comparing the kinetics of histone digestion with each probe relative to the progression of structural collapse (the abrupt transition from turbid nuclear suspension to clear solution), the authors identify which histone is most critical for maintaining chromatin superstructure. G. T. P. Saccone is a new collaborator (first author).

Key Findings

  • H1/H5 and H3 co-digest (type I concerted kinetics): In chicken erythrocyte nuclei, histones H1, H5 and H3 all co-digested with each other, meaning their rates of destruction are mutually dependent. Similarly, rat liver H1 and H3 co-digested. This indicates these histones are intimately associated — consistent with cross-linking data from other labs.
  • Core histones (H2) are protected until H3 is largely gone: The digestion of H2A/H2B (aggregated as “H2”) has a definite lag and does not commence until 50–60% of H3 is degraded, irrespective of probe type or nuclei source. H1 vs H2 shows type III (sequential) kinetics in chicken erythrocytes.
  • H3 destruction is the critical factor in structural collapse: When trypsin is enlarged by conjugation to polyacrylamide, the rate of H1/H5 degradation slows to ~50% relative to structural collapse. Yet H3 degradation rate remains unchanged relative to structural collapse (%SC). This strongly suggests H3 degradation is the primary cause of structural collapse, not merely correlated with it.
  • H3 behaves as a “non-core” histone: Despite being part of the octamer core particle, H3 is rapidly digested — comparable in susceptibility to H1/H5 rather than to H2 or H4. This anomalous exposure is significant for chromatin superstructure models.
  • Probe size effect is modest: Increasing trypsin diameter 3–5-fold with polyacrylamide had observable effects on degradation kinetics but did not materially change the order of events. This indicates the initial sites of tryptic attack are in cavities much larger than the diameter of trypsin (4–5 nm) in both rat liver and chicken erythrocyte nuclei.
  • Two classes of nuclear cavities confirmed: The results are consistent with previous passive penetration studies showing two broad classes of cavities: ~4–5 nm and ~10–15 nm. This places constraints on solenoidal chromatin models.
  • Structural collapse is abrupt: Defined as the point when the digestion mixture changes from turbid nuclear suspension to a clear solution containing an aggregated clump — an abrupt, possibly cascade-like transition.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper complements the DNase I probe studies by using the same size-selective probe logic on the protein component of chromatin rather than the DNA. The identification of H3 as the critical histone for maintaining chromatin superstructure is an important finding — it shows that the octamer “core” is not a monolithic unit but has specific exposed elements that connect to higher-order structure. The novel PA-trypsin probes represent an extension of the Burgoyne lab’s probe-engineering programme from nucleases to proteases. The constraint that initial attack sites must be in cavities >4–5 nm provides useful structural information about chromatin fibre geometry.
