Higher Failures of Amelogenin Sex Test in an Indian Population Group

Authors: Yuet Meng Chang, Leigh A. Burgoyne, Katrin Both

Citations: Well-cited in forensic genetics literature

Phase: Phase 4 — DNA Forensics, FTA Technology & Applied Biology

Context & Background

The amelogenin sex test is the standard method for determining gender in forensic DNA analysis. The test relies on a 6-base-pair deletion between the X and Y chromosome copies of the amelogenin gene (a gene coding for a tooth enamel protein). In commercial forensic kits like AmpFSTR Profiler Plus, males show both X (106 bp) and Y (112 bp) bands, while females show only the X band. Crucially, if the Y copy fails to amplify, a male sample will be incorrectly genotyped as female, which can have serious consequences in criminal investigations, paternity testing, and prenatal diagnosis.

This paper emerged from the collaborative relationship between Yuet Meng Chang (a Malaysian forensic scientist who came to Flinders University to work with Burgoyne) and the Malaysian Chemistry Department's forensic laboratory. Chang's 2002 paper with Burgoyne and Both on Y-STR variation in Asian populations had noted occasional amelogenin dropout in some samples. This 2003 study was designed to systematically investigate the frequency and nature of amelogenin failures across three major ethnic groups in Malaysia. The work also involved a real casework sample from a murder investigation, demonstrating the practical forensic implications of amelogenin failures.

All DNA samples were stored and processed using FTA paper technology (Burgoyne's 1994 invention), showing the integration of Burgoyne's applied DNA preservation innovation with forensic population genetics.

Key Findings

  • Population-specific amelogenin failure rates: In a study of 338 unrelated Malaysian males (113 Malays, 113 Chinese, 112 Indians), amelogenin Y-allele dropout occurred in 3.6% of Indian males (4/112), 0.88% of Malays (1/113), and 0% of Chinese (0/113). The 3.6% failure rate in the Indian group is alarmingly high for a routine forensic test.
  • Casework failure confirmed male misidentification risk: A forensic bloodstain from a murder weapon (severely burnt victim) showed complete amelogenin Y dropout. The sample would have been genotyped as female based on amelogenin alone, but Y-STR analysis confirmed it came from the male victim, matching his muscle tissue DNA profile.
  • Large Y-chromosome deletion, not point mutation: Alternative amelogenin primers (AMEL-2F/3R) spanning the full primer-binding region failed to amplify the Y-allele in all five amelogenin-null males, ruling out point mutations at primer-binding sites. The deletion was confirmed to involve a substantial chromosomal region.
  • MSY1 minisatellite also deleted: All five amelogenin-null males also showed dropout of the MSY1 (DYF155S1) male-specific minisatellite, which is located on the same Y chromosome short arm (Yp) as amelogenin but several megabases distant. This indicated a deletion spanning at least 1 megabase, consistent with the findings of Thangaraj et al. (2002) who reported ~1 Mb deletions in Indian males with amelogenin nulls.
  • Stable amelogenin-null/DYS438*9 haplotype in Indians: All four Indian amelogenin-null males carried the DYS438*allele 9 Y-STR marker, as did two amelogenin-null Sri Lankan Indian males from a previous study (Santos et al. 1998). The DYS438*9 frequency in the studied Indian population was 35%, but its 100% association with amelogenin nulls suggests this haplotype is ancient and stable, possibly representing a founder deletion event in the Indian subcontinent population.
  • Y-STR confirmation of male genotype: Three distant Y-STR loci (DYS438, DYS390, DYS439) successfully amplified in all amelogenin-null samples, including the degraded casework bloodstain. This confirmed male genotype and demonstrated that Y-STRs provide a reliable backup for sex determination when amelogenin fails.
  • FTA technology enables direct amplification: DNA samples stored on FTA paper were amplified directly without extraction (using a simple wash protocol: two washes with 10 mM NaOH, two washes with 0.1X TE, drying). One 1.1 mm FTA disk was sufficient for PCR, demonstrating the practical forensic utility of Burgoyne's FTA platform for population studies and casework.

Significance & Contribution

Forensic impact: This paper provided the first systematic population-level documentation of amelogenin failure rates in Malaysian ethnic groups, and the first casework demonstration of amelogenin failure leading to potential male misidentification. The 3.6% failure rate in the Indian population is far too high to ignore, and the paper made a compelling case for incorporating Y-STR markers into routine forensic sex determination, particularly for populations of South Asian origin.

Population genetics contribution: The identification of a stable amelogenin-null/DYS438*9 haplotype in Indian males provided evidence for an ancient deletion event and demonstrated the utility of combining STR haplotyping with gene deletion analysis for understanding Y-chromosome evolution and population history. The finding was consistent with independent studies from Sri Lanka (Santos et al. 1998) and India (Thangaraj et al. 2002), reinforcing the population specificity of this deletion.

Practical recommendations: The paper advocated for the inclusion of Y-STR loci in forensic multiplexes to prevent gender misidentification, and recommended that laboratories serving populations with Indian ancestry should be aware of the elevated amelogenin failure risk. The authors noted that amelogenin failures could be particularly problematic in prenatal sex testing, paternity cases, and criminal investigations where victim statements conflict with apparent DNA evidence.

FTA platform validation: The successful use of FTA paper for storing, shipping, and directly amplifying 338 population samples, plus a degraded casework bloodstain, provided further validation of Burgoyne's FTA technology in large-scale forensic population genetics studies.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Burgoyne (1994) FTA patent: All DNA samples stored on FTA paper and amplified directly using standard FTA processing protocol
  • Chang, Both & Burgoyne (2002): Y-STR variation study where amelogenin null alleles were first noted in Malaysian populations, prompting this systematic investigation
  • Santos et al. (1998) Nature Genetics: First report of amelogenin Y deletion in two Sri Lankan Indian males; independent support for population-specific deletion
  • Thangaraj et al. (2002) Int J Legal Med: Reported 1.85% amelogenin failure rate in 270 Indian males from India, with ~1 Mb Yp deletion; corroborated the Indian population specificity
  • Roffey et al. (2000) J Forensic Sci: Reported rare amelogenin mutation in an Australian Caucasian; demonstrated that amelogenin failures are not restricted to Indian populations but are rare in Europeans
  • Shewale et al. (2000) Forensic Sci Comm: Reported converse amelogenin X-null alleles (3/7220 males, 0.04%) caused by point mutation, contrasting with the large deletion causing Y-nulls
  • Jobling et al. (1998): MSY1 minisatellite hypervariability for paternal lineage studies; primers adapted for this study to test for co-deletion with amelogenin
  • Ayub et al. (2000): Y-chromosome STR identification and characterization; source of DYS438 and DYS439 primer sequences

What It Contributed To

  • First systematic population study documenting 3.6% amelogenin failure rate in Malaysian Indians, establishing this as a significant forensic concern rather than a rare anomaly
  • First casework demonstration of amelogenin failure in a murder investigation, showing real-world consequences of relying solely on amelogenin for sex determination
  • Confirmation of stable amelogenin-null/DYS438*9 haplotype in Indian populations, suggesting ancient founder deletion event
  • Molecular characterization showing >1 Mb deletion spanning amelogenin and MSY1 loci on Yp (consistent with Thangaraj et al. findings)
  • Practical validation of Y-STR triplex (DYS438, DYS390, DYS439) as reliable backup for sex determination in amelogenin-null samples
  • Recommendation for forensic laboratories serving Indian populations to incorporate Y-STR markers in routine sex testing protocols
  • Large-scale demonstration of FTA technology for population genetics studies (338 samples) and degraded casework (burnt victim bloodstain)
  • Contributed to growing international awareness of amelogenin unreliability, influencing forensic kit design and laboratory protocols
  • Connected to subsequent amelogenin deletion studies, including 2007 Chang et al. paper on amelogenin-negative males in forensic casework

Key Collaborators

Yuet Meng Chang (first author) — Scientific Officer at the Malaysian Chemistry Department, Forensic Laboratory. Chang came to Flinders University to work with Burgoyne on forensic DNA applications, particularly Y-STR analysis and amelogenin studies in Asian populations. This collaboration produced three papers between 2002-2003 on Y-STR haplotypes and amelogenin failures, bridging Malaysian forensic casework with Burgoyne's expertise in DNA technology and FTA platforms. Chang's access to large population datasets from Malaysia's multi-ethnic society enabled systematic investigation of population-specific genetic variation relevant to forensic interpretation.

Katrin Both (third author) — Senior Forensic Scientist at the Forensic Science Center, Adelaide. Co-author on multiple papers with Burgoyne and Chang during this period.
