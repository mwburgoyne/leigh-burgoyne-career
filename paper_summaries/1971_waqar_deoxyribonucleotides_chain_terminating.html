Deoxyribonucleic Acid Synthesis in Mammalian Nuclei: Incorporation of Deoxyribonucleotides and Chain-Terminating Nucleotide Analogues

Authors: M. A. Waqar, L. A. Burgoyne, M. R. Atkinson*

Journal: Biochemical Journal, 121, 803–809

Year: 1971

DOI: 10.1042/bj1210803

Citations: ~40–60 (definitive full paper in a major journal characterising the nuclear DNA synthesis system)

DNA Synthesis Chain Terminators Arabinosyl Nucleotides Nearest-Neighbour Analysis Primer Sites

* M. R. Atkinson — Deceased 30 May 1970. This paper, received by the journal on 7 September 1970, was one of the last to carry Atkinson's name. His death during this period of highly productive research was a significant loss to the Flinders group.

Context & Background

This is the definitive full-length paper characterising the nuclear DNA synthesis system that Burgoyne, Waqar and Atkinson had described in their 1970 BBRC communications. Published in the Biochemical Journal, a more prestigious venue, it provides detailed nearest-neighbour analysis, establishes arabinosyl nucleotides as chain terminators in the nuclear context, and addresses the fundamental question of whether the observed in vitro synthesis reflects true replication or repair. The paper also explores whether nuclei from regenerating liver differ from resting liver in their intrinsic DNA synthesis capacity.

Key Findings

  • Rigorous template-directed synthesis confirmed: Nearest-neighbour analysis after incorporation of 32P-labelled dATP, dCTP, dGTP, dTTP, araATP, and araCTP all showed frequencies consistent with template-dependent copying of rat DNA. No significant homopolymer formation, ruling out terminal transferase activity as a major component.
  • araAMP and araCMP are DNA chain terminators: Both arabinosyl analogues were incorporated into nuclear DNA to a limit determined by the number of available primer sites. When primer sites were exhausted, incorporation stopped; when new sites were created by adding Ca2+ or activated DNA, incorporation resumed. This is the definitive demonstration that both analogues function as chain terminators in mammalian nuclear DNA.
  • Arabinosyl analogues compete with normal nucleotides: araCTP competed successfully with dCTP, and araATP with dATP, for incorporation by the nuclear DNA polymerase. This was significant for understanding the mechanism of action of the anti-cancer drugs arabinosyl cytosine and arabinosyl adenine.
  • Primer site availability controls synthesis: Further evidence that DNA synthesis in the nuclear system was primarily limited by the availability of 3′-hydroxyl priming ends, not by polymerase activity or nucleotide supply. Incorporation from single labelled triphosphates (without the other three) still proceeded at ~50% of the complete rate, interpreted as limit attachment at endogenous primer termini.
  • No significant difference between resting and regenerating liver: Nuclei from normal and 24-hour regenerating rat liver showed no large differences in priming potentiality or intrinsic DNA polymerase activity. The authors concluded honestly: "the processes we are observing in vitro are still a very imperfect reflection of the process of DNA synthesis, or repair, in vivo."

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This is the definitive characterisation paper for the nuclear DNA synthesis system. The comprehensive nearest-neighbour analysis tables provided the community with confident evidence that the nuclear system supported bona fide template-directed synthesis. The demonstration that arabinosyl nucleotides function as chain terminators in intact mammalian nuclei was important for understanding the anti-cancer mechanism of ara-C and ara-A, two clinically important drugs. The honest conclusion that in vitro synthesis did not faithfully reflect in vivo replication showed scientific rigour.

Career significance: This paper carries the poignant note that M. R. Atkinson died on 30 May 1970, during the preparation of this work. Atkinson had been Burgoyne's colleague at Adelaide and a co-author on all the nuclear DNA synthesis papers. His death left Burgoyne and Waqar to continue the programme independently, which they did productively. The publication in the Biochemical Journal (a more substantial venue than BBRC) demonstrates the maturation of their research programme.

  • Momparler (1969): araCTP as chain terminator with purified animal DNA polymerase

  • Josse, Kaiser & Kornberg (1961): Nearest-neighbour analysis methodology

What It Contributed To

  • Understanding of ara-C and ara-A anti-cancer drug mechanisms in nuclear context
  • Chalmers, Atkinson, Burgoyne & Murray (1971): Companion paper on preformed purine utilisation
  • Continued development of the nuclear system leading to chromatin substructure discovery
  • Recognition that in vitro nuclear DNA synthesis reflects repair rather than true replication

Key Collaborators

M. A. Waqar — First author, DNA biochemist at Flinders. M. R. Atkinson — Senior biochemist who died 30 May 1970 during the course of this work. Atkinson had connections to Arthur Kornberg's laboratory and brought expertise in nucleotide analogue biochemistry.
