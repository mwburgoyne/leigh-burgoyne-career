BBC Radio Three: Francis Crick Describes the Hewish & Burgoyne Chromatin Breakthrough (1974)

Programme: Scientifically Speaking, BBC Radio Three

Broadcast Date: 25 March 1974

Presenter: John Maddox

Speakers: Dr. Francis Crick (MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology, Cambridge) and Dr. Morton Bradbury (Dept of Physics, Portsmouth Polytechnic)

Source: BBC transcript purchased by Dr. L. A. Burgoyne for £3.00 + VAT during his study leave at the National Institute for Medical Research, Mill Hill, London (1973–74). BBC cover letter dated 26 April 1974.

Type: External recognition — this is not a Burgoyne publication but a BBC broadcast in which a Nobel laureate explicitly credits the Hewish & Burgoyne discovery

NOTABLE RECOGNITION BBC Radio Chromatin Substructure Nobel Legacy Francis Crick Nucleosome

Context: The Broadcast

Scientifically Speaking was a regular BBC Radio Three programme presented by John Maddox, then a prominent science journalist and former editor of Nature. This episode, broadcast on 25 March 1974, brought together two scientists to discuss the emerging picture of chromosome structure: Francis Crick, co-discoverer of the DNA double helix and 1962 Nobel laureate, and Morton Bradbury, a biophysicist working on chromatin conformation. The conversation ranged across the state of knowledge about how DNA is organised inside living cells — a subject that was, in early 1974, in the midst of a revolution.

That revolution had been triggered by a paper published barely a year earlier. In 1973, Dean Hewish and Leigh Burgoyne at Flinders University had reported in Biochemical and Biophysical Research Communications that an endogenous nuclear enzyme digested chromatin DNA at regularly spaced sites, producing a discrete ladder of DNA fragments — the first experimental evidence that chromatin has a repeating subunit structure. By the time of this broadcast, the implications of that discovery were becoming clear, and Crick used the BBC programme to describe the finding to a national audience.

The Crick Quote: “A Breakthrough”

“But it turns out there has been a breakthrough in this subject made by two Australians, Hewish and Burgoyne. Hewish was a research student of Burgoyne's. And they discovered, they were trying to study something else, DNA replication, they discovered that there is a sub-unit structure which you can show chemically in chromosomes and now we know in chromatin. What they find essentially is that under special conditions they can activate an enzyme which is in the nucleii of certain cells, which begins to break down the DNA. You let this enzyme digest it for a period, and then you extract the DNA and look at its size and you get the astonishing result that you don't get a whole distribution of size as you would if the whole DNA was just nibbling here and there. You put this on a gell which spreads it out according to size, you see a band with 200 base pairs, you see another one with 400, another with 600, 800, going up, it's clearly quantised. It isn't exactly 200.”
— Dr. Francis Crick, BBC Radio Three, Scientifically Speaking, 25 March 1974 (p. 9 of transcript)

Note Crick's wording: he calls the Hewish & Burgoyne result a “breakthrough” and an “astonishing result.” He explains the key observation — the quantised DNA ladder at multiples of ~200 base pairs — clearly and accurately, and correctly identifies the serendipitous nature of the discovery: Hewish and Burgoyne had been studying DNA replication, not chromatin structure. The enzyme they activated was the Ca2+-Mg2+ endonuclease that Burgoyne's group had characterised since 1970.

Why This Matters

A Nobel laureate's public endorsement: Francis Crick was not merely a distinguished scientist; he was a co-discoverer of the double helix and recipient of the 1962 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. For Crick to describe the Hewish & Burgoyne work as a “breakthrough” on national radio, explaining it in detail and by name, is an extraordinary form of recognition. This was BBC Radio Three reaching a broad UK audience, not a specialist seminar.

The discovery Crick described: The 1973 Hewish & Burgoyne paper in BBRC (~1,172 citations) provided the first direct experimental evidence for a repeating subunit in chromatin. The discrete DNA ladder produced by endogenous nuclease digestion showed that DNA in chromosomes is not randomly packaged but is organised in a periodic architecture. This observation was foundational to what followed.

What followed — the nucleosome model: Within months of this broadcast, Roger Kornberg proposed the nucleosome model (Science, 1974), building on the experimental evidence that Hewish & Burgoyne had provided. The Olins & Olins team visualised “nu bodies” (beads on a string) by electron microscopy (Science, 1974), and Markus Noll confirmed the ~200 bp repeat with micrococcal nuclease (Nature, 1974). All three groups cited Hewish & Burgoyne. Roger Kornberg went on to receive the 2006 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on the molecular basis of eukaryotic transcription — work built upon the understanding of chromatin organisation that the Hewish & Burgoyne discovery helped establish.

Hewish & Burgoyne's experiment provided key evidence for the chromatin subunit model that would later form part of the scientific foundation recognised by Kornberg's 2006 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. The chain of influence: Hewish & Burgoyne's experiment → Kornberg's nucleosome model → decades of chromatin biology → Nobel recognition.

The Timing: Mill Hill Study Leave

The BBC's cover letter, dated 26 April 1974, was addressed to Dr. L. A. Burgoyne at the National Institute for Medical Research, Mill Hill, London NW7 1AA. Leigh was on his first major study leave (1973–74, nine months), working with J. R. Tata at MRC Mill Hill. He was therefore in the UK and able to hear or learn of the broadcast in real time. Leigh paid £3.00 plus VAT for the transcript — and kept it for over fifty years, suggesting how much Crick's on-air acknowledgment meant to him.

It is worth noting the geography: Crick was at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge, a short distance from Mill Hill. Leigh was embedded in the MRC ecosystem during precisely the period when his discovery was transforming chromatin biology.

Connections

  • The discovery Crick describes: Hewish & Burgoyne (1973) — BBRC 52(2), 504–510. Chromatin sub-structure: regularly spaced nuclease digestion sites. ~1,172 citations.
  • The companion endonuclease paper: Hewish & Burgoyne (1973) — Characterisation of the Ca-Mg endonuclease.
  • The 2D gel follow-up: Burgoyne & Hewish (1974) — Two-dimensional polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis confirmation of repeating substructure.
  • Kornberg (1974): Science 184, 868–871. “Chromatin structure: a repeating unit of histones and DNA.” Proposed the nucleosome model, citing Hewish & Burgoyne.
  • Olins & Olins (1974): Science 183, 330–332. Electron microscopic visualisation of “nu bodies” (beads on a string), citing Hewish & Burgoyne.
  • Noll (1974): Nature 251, 249–251. Micrococcal nuclease ladder confirming ~200 bp repeat, citing Hewish & Burgoyne.
  • Kornberg Nobel Prize (2006): Nobel Prize in Chemistry for “studies of the molecular basis of eukaryotic transcription” — built on the chromatin structural understanding to which Hewish & Burgoyne's discovery contributed foundational evidence.

About the Speakers

Francis Crick (1916–2004) — Co-discoverer of the DNA double helix with James Watson (1953). Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1962. At the time of this broadcast he was at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology, Cambridge, where he had moved beyond DNA structure to study chromatin and gene regulation. His willingness to describe the Hewish & Burgoyne result as a “breakthrough” on national radio reflects the impact the discovery had on the field.

Morton Bradbury — Biophysicist at the Department of Physics, Portsmouth Polytechnic. His research focused on the physical characterisation of histone interactions and chromatin conformation, complementing the biochemical approach of the Hewish & Burgoyne experiments.

John Maddox (1925–2009) — Programme presenter. Science journalist and editor of Nature (1966–1973, and again 1980–1995). One of the most influential science communicators of his era.
