Purification and Properties of the Aromatic (arom) Synthetic Enzyme Aggregate of Neurospora crassa

Authors: Leigh Burgoyne, Mary E. Case, Norman H. Giles

Journal: Biochimica et Biophysica Acta — Enzymology, 191(2), 452–462

Year: 1969

DOI: 10.1016/0005-2744(69)90248-2

Citations: ~80–120 (significant; first purification of an important model multienzyme complex)

Enzyme Purification Multienzyme Complex Neurospora Aromatic Biosynthesis Protein Structure

Context & Background

The arom gene cluster in Neurospora crassa encodes a remarkable multienzyme aggregate carrying five sequential enzyme activities in aromatic amino acid biosynthesis (steps 2–6 of the pre-chorismic acid pathway). Previous genetic work by Giles and colleagues had characterised the gene cluster, but the protein complex itself had not been purified. This paper — with Burgoyne as first author — provides the first purification to near-homogeneity and the definitive physical characterisation of this pentafunctional enzyme.

Key Findings

  • Purification to near-homogeneity: Developed an 11-step purification procedure from N. crassa mycelium involving spermine precipitation, ammonium sulphate fractionation, ion exchange (K+ Biorad, DEAE-Sephadex), calcium phosphate gel, and Sepharose 4B gel filtration. The purified enzyme showed a single major peak in the analytical ultracentrifuge.
  • Molecular weight: Determined as ~231,000 by equilibrium sedimentation combined with amino acid analysis (v̄ = 0.74). Sedimentation coefficient s20,w = 11.4 S.
  • Inseparable five activities: All five enzyme activities co-purified through every step and co-sedimented in sucrose gradients — no conditions separated them during purification.
  • Dimer of identical halves: SDS treatment and SDS-PAGE indicated dissociation to a single species of ~100,000 MW. Proposed the complex consists of two identical halves joined by weak non-peptide bonds.
  • Smaller subunits detected: Under more extreme conditions (guanidine-HCl), species ranging from 29,000 to 120,000 MW were detected, possibly representing individual polypeptide chains.
  • Reactivation possible: Three of five activities (DHS reductase, dehydroquinase, shikimate kinase) could be reactivated after denaturation by boiling, urea, guanidine, or SDS treatment — indicating these catalytic sites can refold independently.
  • Oxygen sensitivity: The enzyme was readily inactivated by O2 even in the presence of thiols, requiring anaerobic (N2) handling throughout.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This was the first purification and physical characterisation of one of the earliest-recognised multienzyme complexes (now known to be a multidomain/multifunctional protein). The arom complex became a textbook example of gene cluster organisation and multifunctional enzymes. Later work would show that in fungi this is actually a single large polypeptide chain carrying all five domains — making it one of the first recognised multifunctional proteins. Burgoyne's proposal of two identical halves was prescient.

Career significance: This is arguably the most significant paper from Burgoyne's Neurospora period. As first author on a paper that would become a foundational reference for the arom system, it demonstrates his strength in demanding protein purification work. The 11-step protocol requiring strict anaerobic conditions shows the same practical ingenuity that characterised his cytochrome b2 work and would later emerge in FTA card development.

Key Collaborators
