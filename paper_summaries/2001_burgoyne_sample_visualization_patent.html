Context & Background

By 2001, the core FTA card patent family comprised seven granted US patents covering the composition, storage, analysis, and release of genetic material on dry solid media. These patents had established the chemical formulations (Tris–urate buffer, EDTA chelation, SDS denaturation) and use methods for FTA cards. However, a practical problem remained unsolved in the patent literature: when a nearly colourless biological sample such as saliva, urine, or cerebrospinal fluid was applied to FTA paper, the collector could not visually confirm where — or whether — the sample had been deposited. This created three real-world problems: (1) biosafety exposure hazard for lab personnel handling samples containing human pathogens without knowing which areas were contaminated, (2) incomplete sample retrieval when punching discs from the card for analysis, and (3) logistical chaos at centralised facilities receiving hundreds of cards with invisible sample spots. This patent application addresses all three problems by incorporating a visible tracer system directly into the dry solid medium.

Key Claims & Innovations

  • Claim 1 — Broadest visualization claim: A dry solid medium for storing biological samples comprising a material that enables visualization of the applied sample, where that material does not interfere with subsequent analysis. This is the broadest claim, covering any visualization approach.
  • Claims 2–7 — Visible material specifics: The visualization material comprises a pigment or dye (Claim 2), selected from carbon or metal sols, bromophenol blue, and carminic acid (Claim 3), arranged in a pattern (Claim 4) such as a grid (Claim 5), checkerboard (Claim 6), or dots (Claim 7).
  • Claim 8 — Appearance-alteration claim: A dry solid medium with a visible material whose appearance is altered upon sample application so that the occupied area becomes visible. This captures the key inventive concept — that wetting changes the visual state of the medium.
  • Claims 9–14 — Diffusion-based pattern alteration: The visible material is in a pattern altered by diffusion (Claim 9), comprising inert water-soluble dye (Claim 10), specifically bromophenol blue or carminic acid (Claim 11), in grid (Claim 12), checkerboard (Claim 13), or dot (Claim 14) patterns.
  • Claims 15–16 — Combined visualization + DNA storage: Integration of the visualization system with the protective FTA composition (protein denaturing agent + free radical trap), bridging this invention to the core FTA patent family.
  • Claims 17–18 — Method claims: Two distinct methods of visualization: (17) pre-applied dye that diffuses with the sample, and (18) dye applied after sample deposition to reveal sample position.

Significance & Contribution

Practical innovation: This patent addresses a human-factors gap in the FTA card system. All prior FTA patents focused on the chemistry of DNA preservation, pathogen inactivation, and analytical compatibility. This invention focuses on the user experience — giving the sample collector immediate visual feedback that their sample has been properly deposited and showing exactly where it landed. The core mechanism is elegantly simple: a water-soluble dye printed in a pattern (grid, checkerboard, or dots) on the paper matrix dissolves and diffuses when wetted by a biological sample. The diffused pattern visually marks the sample boundaries against the undisturbed background.

Field collection quality assurance: By 2001, FTA cards were being deployed in field settings for forensic DNA databases, newborn screening, and wildlife biodiversity sampling — often by personnel without laboratory training. The inability to see whether a colourless sample had been properly applied was a significant source of error. This invention provides built-in quality control at the point of collection, without requiring any additional equipment or reagent.

Biosafety significance: The patent explicitly addresses the exposure hazard when handling samples containing human pathogens. Making the sample location visible allows handlers to avoid contact with contaminated regions of the card, a critical safety feature for forensic and clinical applications.

Commercial impact: The indicating dye grid became a standard feature of commercial FTA cards (the coloured circle visible on modern Whatman FTA cards). This patent protects the feature that most users associate with FTA technology — the colour change confirming sample application.
