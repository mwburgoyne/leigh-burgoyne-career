Avian Species-Specific Tandem Repeats Contain Nuclear Protein Binding Sites

Authors: Xiaoyi Zhu, Alessandro Testori, Sawyin Oh, Jenny D. Skinner and Leigh A. Burgoyne

Journal: Biochemical and Biophysical Research Communications, 182(2), 447–451

Year: 1992

Institution: School of Biological Sciences, Flinders University, Adelaide

Received: 6 December 1991

DOI: —

Citations: ~15–25 (estimated)

Tandem Repeat Genus-Specific Nuclear Protein Binding AP2 ATF T Antigen Chicken Goose Swan Avian Genome Repetitive DNA

Context & Background

This paper extends the Zhu/Burgoyne avian repetitive DNA programme by comparing genus-specific tandem repeats from chickens, geese, and swans, and discovering that each contains specific nuclear protein binding sites. Building on the 1990 goose macrosatellite paper and a 1991 Biochemical International paper (Zhu et al. 1991, ref. 16 — characterising Anas-specific and Cairina-specific repeats), this work adds chicken-specific and swan-specific repeats to complete a five-genus comparative survey. The finding of transcription factor binding sites within these repeats bridges the avian genomics programme with the chromatin/DNA-protein interaction themes of Burgoyne’s earlier work.

Key Findings

  • Three genus-specific tandem repeats of similar length (~42–43 bp) but completely different sequences:
    • Chicken-specific (42 bp): Found in Gallus domesticus and Gallus gallus only. Contains T antigen binding site GGGGC, oligo A/T stretches fitting the consensus T/GAAAAT/C (alpha-protein and W-protein binding sites involved in chromatin packaging). 52% homology to the sex-specific W-chromosome repeat. ~1.13% of genome (1.7×106 copies/cell). Zero contribution to other avian genomes.
    • Swan-specific (~43 bp): GC-rich, isolated from White swan (Cygnus olor), also present in Black swan (Cygnus atratus). Divergent copies (76–98% homology) with a conserved 14 bp core GGGGTGGGTCCTCC containing the AP2 transcription factor binding site CCCCACCC. ~3.67% of genome (5.6×106 copies/cell).
    • Goose-specific (43 bp): Previously characterised (Zhu et al. 1990). Present in Toulouse, Emden, and Chinese goose. Well conserved (>95% homology between species). Contains AP2 binding site TCCCCAG, acute phase response element TCACT, and ATF binding sites TCACGTCA and TACGTCA. ~3.15–4.09% of genome.
  • Additional genus-specific repeats: Anas-specific 14 bp repeat (CTGAGGTCACAATG, containing CAAT box and CRE-homologous site) and Cairina-specific 13 bp repeat (GAGGCGCAGAATA, containing T antigen site and RNA promoter element) were also genus-restricted (from Zhu et al. 1991 Biochem. Int.).
  • Nuclear protein binding sites are universal: All five genus-specific repeats contain recognisable transcription factor or nuclear protein binding sites, despite having completely different sequences.
  • Proposed amplification mechanism: Short tandem repeats amplified from ancestral sequences containing nuclear protein binding sites via enzymatic binding, unwinding, nicking, slippage, repair, and ligation.
  • Speculative significance: The genus-specific accumulation of particular binding sites might play a role in determining phenotypic differences between genera.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper made the striking observation that independently evolved, genus-specific tandem repeats in birds all converge on a common feature: the presence of nuclear protein binding sites. This suggests that the amplification and retention of these repeats is not random but is functionally constrained by protein–DNA interactions. The proposed mechanism linking nuclear protein binding to repeat amplification via slippage was an early contribution to understanding the biogenesis of tandem repetitive DNA. The complete five-genus survey (covering two orders — Galliformes and Anseriformes) provided the comparative breadth needed to support the conclusion.

Career significance: This is the culminating paper of the Zhu/Burgoyne avian repetitive DNA series (1990–1992). Xiaoyi Zhu’s full name appears for the first time. Alessandro Testori returns from the PKC and phosphorylation papers. Sawyin Oh is a new collaborator. Jenny D. Skinner’s full name (Jennifer/Jenny) continues. The paper bridges the avian genomics programme with Burgoyne’s long-standing interest in DNA–protein interactions and chromatin packaging. Published in BBRC.
