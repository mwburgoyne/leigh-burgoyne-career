The Use of MSY1 Locus in the Investigation of Mixed Samples

Authors: Yuet Meng Chang, Leigh A. Burgoyne, Katrin Both

Journal: The Forensic Scientist, Vol. 1, No. 1, April 2003, pp. 6–16

Year: 2003

Institutions: School of Biological Sciences, Flinders University of South Australia; Forensic Science Centre, Adelaide, South Australia

Citations: Not indexed in OpenAlex (published in specialized forensic journal)

Phase: Phase 4 — DNA Forensics, FTA Technology & Applied Biology

Context & Background

By 2003, Y-chromosome STR typing had become a valuable forensic tool for analyzing male DNA in sexual assault evidence, particularly when male and female DNA were mixed together. However, conventional autosomal STR multiplexes (like AmpFlSTR Profiler Plus) struggled to discriminate between multiple male contributors in complex mixtures. The Y-chromosome's haploid inheritance pattern (passed unchanged from father to son) made it ideal for isolating male-specific profiles, but additional highly polymorphic markers were needed to differentiate between unrelated males.

The MSY1 locus (DYF155S1), located on the short arm of the Y-chromosome, represented the most variable Y-linked marker discovered to date. It consists of 50–115 tandem repeats of a 25-base-pair core sequence, with at least five variant repeat types creating enormous structural diversity. The conventional approach to typing MSY1 (Minisatellite Variant Repeat PCR or MVR-PCR) produced digital codes mapping the position of each variant repeat along the array — highly informative, but technically demanding and time-consuming for routine forensic casework.

Chang, working as a PhD student under Burgoyne's supervision at Flinders University in collaboration with Katrin Both from the South Australian Forensic Science Centre, developed a faster alternative approach. Instead of mapping the entire repeat array, they targeted the junctions between blocks of different repeat types using semi-nested PCR with junction-specific primers. This generated length polymorphism patterns that could be visualized on standard forensic platforms (ABI PRISM 377 DNA Sequencer with GeneScan software), making MSY1 typing practical for routine casework. Samples were collected and processed using FTA paper — Burgoyne's 1994 invention — demonstrating the integration of his forensic technologies.

Key Findings

  • Modified flanking primer eliminates non-polymorphic homolog: The original MSY1 flanking primers (Y1A+ and Y1B+) amplified both the polymorphic MSY1 locus (2–3 kb) and an intense non-polymorphic homolog (~203 bp). By redesigning the reverse primer (Y1E+ replacing Y1B+) to exploit a single-base difference (A→C at position 998) between MSY1 and its homolog, the team eliminated the interfering product, amplifying only the polymorphic MSY1 band under high-stringency conditions.
  • Three junction primer sets provide complementary discrimination: Semi-nested PCR using one flanking primer plus a junction-specific primer generated different polymorphism patterns: (1) Y1A+/Pr 1,3 targeted type 1&3 junctions (highest polymorphism, threefold variation in smallest fragment, largest fragment, and total band count); (2) Y1A+/Pr 3,1 targeted type 3&1 junctions (fewer bands, not all Y-chromosomes have type 3 repeats at the array beginning); (3) Y1E+/Pr 3,4R targeted type 3&4 junctions in reverse orientation (twofold polymorphism, consistent smallest fragment of 309 bp across all samples).
  • Validation on control sample confirms accuracy: MSY1 control sample M1 (supplied by M.A. Jobling, University of Leicester) with published MVR code of 16 type 1 repeats, 40 type 3 repeats, and 18 type 4 repeats was typed precisely by the semi-nested method: 16 type 1 repeats and 18 type 4 repeats confirmed exactly; type 3 repeat block confirmed as >30 repeats (internal size standard limited to 928 bp prevented exact count).
  • Simple mixtures visually resolved on agarose gels: Simulated two- and three-contributor male mixtures showed MSY1 allelic length variations in accord with individual component samples. The degree of MSY1 amplification reflected relative contribution of each male in the mixture. For highly complex mixtures (500 sperm donors), a broad smear indicated an upper limit on resolvable diversity, but simple mixtures (2–3 contributors) displayed distinct band patterns.
  • Real casework validation demonstrates practical utility: Gang rape case involving three suspects (only one arrested) was analyzed. Profiler Plus autosomal STR typing of differential extract showed mixed profile with >2 contributors and DNA degradation (dropout at D18 and D7 loci). MSY1 typing of non-differential extract (no epithelial/sperm separation) using Y1A+/Pr 1,3 revealed: (a) arrested suspect had unique MSY1 profile with one missed type 1 repeat (displayed 10 continuous bands instead of 11, missing 400 bp band), (b) evidence stain showed arrested suspect's profile as major component mixed with minor profile from another male (presumably one of the unarrested suspects), (c) the 400 bp gap in the mixed profile confirmed the arrested suspect's DNA presence in the evidence.
  • FTA technology enables simple sample processing: Blood samples on FTA paper processed using aqueous FTA method (2 mm disc extraction). Mixed male DNA sample (500 sperm donors) loaded onto pre-washed FTA paper and treated with alkaline-isopropanol to remove polyamines before analysis. Optimal template DNA: 0.5–2.5 ng per amplification.
  • High-stringency PCR conditions critical for success: PCR 1 used low Mg2+, high annealing temperature (95°C, 11 min; 28 cycles at 94°C, 30s / 65°C, 2 min / 68°C, 3 min), and low primer concentration to minimize flanking primer carryover. Extension temperature held at 68°C (not 72°C) to prevent template depurination and lane smearing. PCR 2 used junction primers at 5× the concentration of PCR 1 flanking primers.
  • Mixture patterns show characteristic symmetry/asymmetry: Single-source samples displayed obvious symmetry in peak patterns; mixed samples showed obvious asymmetry. This visual distinction indicates mixtures are "readily identifiable by eye," and suggests that sophisticated deconvolution mathematics (common in mass spectrometry for resolving mixed orderly series of peaks) could be applied to MSY1 mixture resolution in the future.

Significance & Contribution

Forensic innovation: This paper provided the first practical, high-throughput approach to MSY1 typing suitable for routine forensic casework. The semi-nested junction primer method was faster and less technically demanding than conventional MVR-PCR digital coding, while retaining high discriminatory power. The successful application to a real gang rape case demonstrated that MSY1 could resolve mixed male profiles even when autosomal STRs showed complex, degraded mixtures with >2 contributors.

Technical advancement: The modified flanking primer (Y1E+) elegantly solved the non-polymorphic homolog problem by exploiting a single-base difference, demonstrating careful primer design can eliminate interference without resorting to extreme PCR conditions. The three junction primer sets (targeting 1&3, 3&1, and 3&4 junctions) provided complementary information — if males shared similar patterns at one junction, they could often be distinguished at another.

FTA integration: The paper showcased FTA technology (Burgoyne's 1994 patent) as the sample collection and processing method, illustrating how Burgoyne's applied technologies worked together: FTA cards collected and preserved DNA samples; aqueous FTA processing extracted clean DNA without organic solvents; MSY1 junction typing then resolved male contributors in mixed samples. The highly diverse mixed male sample (500 sperm donors) was specifically processed on FTA paper with alkaline-isopropanol treatment to remove polyamines — a refinement of the FTA chemistry.

Career integration: This work exemplifies the collaborative model of Burgoyne's Phase 4 forensic programme: graduate student (Yuet Meng Chang) developing methods under Burgoyne's supervision at Flinders University, validated in partnership with operational forensic laboratory (Katrin Both, Forensic Science Centre Adelaide), presented at the major international forensic meeting (IAFS Montpellier 2002), and published in a specialized forensic journal to reach practitioner audiences.

Impact on mixture analysis: While not heavily cited in OpenAlex (published in a specialized forensic practitioner journal rather than mainstream molecular biology journals), the paper contributed to the growing toolkit for Y-chromosome mixture resolution in sexual assault casework. The acknowledgment that "sophisticated deconvolution mathematics" could further resolve complex MSY1 mixture patterns anticipated the later development of probabilistic genotyping software for complex DNA mixtures.
