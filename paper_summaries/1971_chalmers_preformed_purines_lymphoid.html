Evidence for the Utilization of Preformed Purines by Rat Lymphoid Tissues: Implication to Immunosuppression by 6-Thiopurine Analogues

Authors: A. H. Chalmers, M. R. Atkinson*, L. A. Burgoyne, A. W. Murray

Journal: Australian Journal of Experimental Biology and Medical Science, 49, 177–183

Year: 1971

DOI: 10.1038/icb.1971.19

Citations: ~15–25 (moderate; specialised immunopharmacology)

* M. R. Atkinson — Deceased May 1970

Purine Metabolism Lymphoid Tissue Immunosuppression Azathioprine Nucleotide Biosynthesis

Context & Background

This paper is a collaboration between the University of Adelaide Department of Surgery (Chalmers, Murray) and Flinders University (Burgoyne, Atkinson). It addresses a question in immunopharmacology: do lymphoid tissues synthesise their own purines de novo, or do they depend on preformed purines supplied by the liver? The answer has implications for understanding how immunosuppressive thiopurine drugs (azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine) selectively affect the immune system. This represents a different research direction for Burgoyne — applied nucleotide biochemistry connected to clinical immunosuppression.

Key Findings

  • Portal occlusion selectively inhibits purine incorporation: Ligating the hepatic artery and portal vein (cutting off liver function) selectively inhibited [14C]formate incorporation into DNA purines of rat lymph nodes and thymus, while incorporation into DNA thymine was unaffected. This showed lymphoid tissues depend on the liver for their purine supply.
  • Confirmed in vitro and by perfusion: Both perfused rat head preparations and lymph node slices incubated in vitro showed T/A and T/G ratios increased 6–9 fold over control animals, confirming that isolated lymphoid tissue has very limited de novo purine synthesis capacity.
  • Immunosuppressive drugs mimic portal occlusion: Both azathioprine and its 9-butyl derivative significantly increased T/A and T/G ratios in spleen, lymph nodes and thymus — the same pattern as portal occlusion. This suggested that inhibition of purine supply is a significant component of immunosuppressive drug action.
  • Different mechanisms for the two drugs: 9-Butylazathioprine and its metabolite do not inhibit the key de novo purine synthesis enzyme (PRPP amidotransferase), unlike azathioprine metabolites, suggesting a different mechanism of action despite similar immunosuppressive outcomes.
  • Partial hepatectomy less effective: 70–75% hepatectomy produced only small effects on purine incorporation, probably because the remaining liver tissue continued de novo purine biosynthesis.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper provided evidence for the tissue-specific dependence of lymphoid organs on liver-derived preformed purines, extending earlier work on bone marrow. The connection to immunosuppressive drug mechanisms was clinically relevant — azathioprine was (and remains) widely used in transplantation medicine. The elegant experimental design using portal occlusion, head perfusion, and in vitro incubation provided convergent evidence for the same conclusion.

Career significance: This is a more peripheral paper for Burgoyne — he is third of four authors, and the work is primarily driven by Chalmers and Murray at Adelaide. However, it illustrates the continuing Adelaide–Flinders connection and Burgoyne's breadth beyond the Ca2+-dependent endonuclease programme. The paper also carries the poignant note of Atkinson's death (May 1970), reflecting the ongoing impact of that loss on the collaborative network.
