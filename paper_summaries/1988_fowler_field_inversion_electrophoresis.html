Improved Separation of Multi-Locus Hypervariable DNA Restriction Fragments by Field Inversion Gel Electrophoresis and Fragment Detection Using a Biotinylated Probe

Authors: J. Craig S. Fowler, Jenny D. Skinner, Leigh A. Burgoyne & James L. McInnes

Year: 1988

Received: 15 March 1988  |  Accepted: 2 July 1988

Citations: 6 (OpenAlex)

Phase: Phase 3 — Chromatin Structure & Higher-Order Organisation

Context & Background

By 1988, the Fowler-Skinner-Burgoyne collaboration at Flinders had characterised the remarkable human Satellite III polymorphism system — highly variable tandem repeat DNA showing extreme length variation at multiple chromosomal loci. This system held great promise for forensic identification and parentage testing, but there was a practical problem: the large restriction fragments (many exceeding 20 kb) did not separate well on conventional agarose gels, making individual alleles difficult to resolve.

At the same time, field inversion gel electrophoresis (FIGE) was emerging as a technique for separating very large DNA molecules by periodically reversing the electric field direction. This paper applied FIGE to the Satellite III system and simultaneously tested a non-isotopic biotinylated probe for fragment detection. The combination addressed two limitations of forensic DNA typing: poor resolution of large fragments and the safety and regulatory burden of radioactive probes.

Key Findings

  • FIGE extends linear separation range to >48 kb: Conventional agarose gel electrophoresis produced linear separation (on log kb plots) only up to about 10 kb. Field inversion gel electrophoresis extended this to at least 48.5 kb, dramatically improving resolution of the large hypervariable Satellite III fragments.
  • Successful application to complex TaqI/Satellite III polymorphism: FIGE clearly resolved multiple large Satellite III alleles in human genomic DNA digested with TaqI, a system too complex for conventional gels. The improved separation made individual bands distinguishable and interpretable.
  • Biotinylated probe nearly as sensitive as radioactive probe: The 228S satellite probe was biotinylated and detected using streptavidin-alkaline phosphatase with chemiluminescent substrate. Sensitivity approached that of [35S]-labelled probe, offering a safer alternative for forensic laboratories.
  • Y-chromosome detection as a bonus: The biotinylated 228S probe also detected the 3.4 kb Y-specific restriction fragment, providing simultaneous sex identification — a useful feature for forensic casework.
  • Explicit forensic application discussed: The paper explicitly framed the work in forensic terms, noting the value of improved hypervariable fragment resolution for parentage testing and forensic identification. This is one of the earliest Flinders satellite DNA papers to emphasise practical forensic applications rather than basic chromosome biology.

Significance & Contribution

Technical significance: This paper bridged fundamental satellite DNA research and applied forensic technology. FIGE was not widely adopted in forensic laboratories (eventually overtaken by PCR-based STR profiling in the 1990s), but the paper demonstrated that the Flinders group was actively exploring practical solutions to real forensic DNA typing challenges.

Non-isotopic probe development: The biotinylated probe work, conducted in collaboration with James McInnes at the Adelaide Gene Technology Centre, showed that non-radioactive detection could achieve near-radioactive sensitivity — an important step for forensic lab implementation where radioactive waste disposal and licensing were significant barriers.

Y-chromosome marker thread: The incidental detection of the Y-specific 3.4 kb fragment foreshadows the later forensic Y-chromosome work (e.g., 2007 Y-STR amelogenin paper), demonstrating the group’s awareness of sex determination markers as a forensic tool.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Fowler, Drinkwater, Burgoyne & Skinner (1987): First characterisation of Satellite III hypervariable length polymorphism — the system being optimised here
  • Fowler et al. (1988a, in press): Human satellite-III DNA as a macrosatellite polymorphism — detailed Satellite III structure and chromosomal distribution
  • Fowler et al. (1988b, in press, J Forensic Sci): Genomic localisation of Satellite III sequences — mapping to chromosomes 1, 9, and 16
  • Carle & Olson (1985): Alternating-field gel electrophoresis technique for large DNA separation
  • Southern (1975): Detection of specific DNA sequences by blotting and hybridisation — the foundational Southern blot method
  • Jeffreys, Wilson & Thein (1985): Hypervariable minisatellite regions for DNA fingerprinting — established forensic value of hypervariable DNA

What It Contributed To

  • Fowler & Burgoyne (1989): Satellite III TaqI-deficient polymorphic sequences — continued refinement of the Satellite III system
  • 1989 satellite DNA and primate phylogeny: Extended the Satellite III comparative genomics thread
  • 1994 FTA patent: Part of the forensic DNA technology development trajectory that led to the FTA paper blood collection system — both FIGE and FTA addressed practical barriers to forensic DNA analysis
  • 2007 Y-STR amelogenin paper: Y-specific marker detection thread continues with modern STR technology
  • Demonstrated practical forensic problem-solving mindset that characterised the transition from Phase 3 (basic satellite DNA biology) to Phase 4 (applied forensic technology)
  • Contributed to the broader shift in forensic DNA labs away from radioactive probes toward non-isotopic detection methods in the late 1980s and early 1990s

Key Collaborators

J. Craig S. Fowler — Lead author, PhD student/postdoc at Flinders, central to the Satellite III characterisation programme (1987–1989). This is his third major Satellite III paper.

Jenny D. Skinner — Long-term Burgoyne collaborator at Flinders (chromatin superstructure work from 1981, satellite DNA from 1987). Essential technical expertise in DNA Southern blotting and probe preparation.

James L. McInnes — University of Adelaide, Commonwealth Special Research Centre for Gene Technology. Provided the biotinylated probe technology and non-isotopic detection expertise, extending the Flinders-Adelaide collaboration into applied molecular biology techniques.
