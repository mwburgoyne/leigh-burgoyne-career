Chromatin Structure: A Property of the Higher Structure of Chromatin and the Time Course of Its Formation During Chromatin Replication

Authors: L. A. Burgoyne, J. D. Mobbs, A. J. Marshall

Journal: Nucleic Acids Research, 3(12), 3293–3304

Year: 1976

Received: 13 September 1976

DOI: —

Citations: ~40–70 (estimated)

Chromatin Superstructure DNase I Resistance Chromatin Replication Nucleosome Packing Maturation Higher-Order Structure

Context & Background

By 1976, the nucleosome as the basic repeating unit of chromatin was well established. The next frontier was understanding how nucleosomes are packed into higher-order structures. This paper shifts Burgoyne's focus from the nucleosome level to chromatin superstructure — using differential nuclease susceptibility as a probe. The key insight is that DNase I resistance reveals a level of organisation above the nucleosome: a fragile but highly protective packing arrangement. The paper also introduces an elegant pulse-labelling approach to follow the kinetics of chromatin maturation after DNA replication in regenerating rat liver. A. J. Marshall appears as a new collaborator.

Key Findings

  • Resting chromatin is highly resistant to DNase I: Over 90 min of DNase I digestion, only ~0.5% of DNA was rendered acid-soluble in intact nuclei. The bulk DNA remained >800 bp. This is a near-total resistance at the genome level.
  • Trypsin destroys this resistance: When trypsin was added alongside DNase I, the DNA became rapidly and extensively degraded (~20% acid-soluble by 90 min). Trypsin removes histone H1 and modifies other histones, destroying the higher-order packing that protects against DNase I.
  • Calcium also destroys the resistance: Adding 1 mM Ca2+ (replacing EGTA) dramatically increased DNase I susceptibility (~40% acid-soluble by 90 min), possibly via Ca-Mg endonuclease action, Ca2+-stimulated proteases, or direct effects on DNase I catalysis.
  • Micrococcal nuclease is NOT hindered by superstructure: In sharp contrast to DNase I, micrococcal nuclease attacked resting chromatin rapidly and trypsin had virtually no effect on its action. This means the superstructure specifically blocks DNase I (MW 31,000, ~41 Å diameter) but not micrococcal nuclease (MW 16,800, ~34 Å diameter) — suggesting a packing system with extremely uniform gap dimensions.
  • Newly synthesized chromatin is transiently DNase I susceptible: Using intraportal 3H-thymidine pulse labelling in regenerating rat liver, newly synthesized chromatin (3.2 min post-pulse) was as susceptible to DNase I as trypsinised chromatin. This susceptibility decreased rapidly over ~10 min to the near-complete resistance of mature chromatin.
  • Three-stage maturation model proposed: Integrating with Weintraub's work on chick chromatin, Burgoyne proposed: (1) a very short initial DNase I-resistant phase (Weintraub), (2) a longer DNase I-susceptible phase (~3–10 min, this paper), (3) the final highly DNase I-resistant state of mature organised chromatin.
  • Size-exclusion model for superstructure: The differential susceptibility to DNase I vs micrococcal nuclease suggests nucleosomes are packed so that each protects its neighbours from molecules >~41 Å but not from molecules <~34 Å. This implies extremely uniform inter-nucleosome spacing in the higher-order structure.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper represents an important conceptual advance — moving beyond the nucleosome itself to ask how nucleosomes are packed in three dimensions. The differential nuclease susceptibility approach (DNase I vs micrococcal nuclease, +/− trypsin) was an elegant experimental strategy. The chromatin maturation kinetics experiment was technically demanding (intraportal labelling, rapid sacrifice, nuclear isolation) and provided one of the first direct measurements of higher-order chromatin assembly time after replication. The similarity to Seale's findings in HeLa cells (1975) indicated this was a general phenomenon.

Career significance: This paper marks the beginning of Burgoyne's sustained programme on chromatin higher-order structure that would continue through the 1980s. A. J. Marshall appears as a new collaborator — the first appearance of this name in the series. The paper also demonstrates Burgoyne's characteristic approach: using multiple enzyme probes on the same preparation to deduce structural features by differential sensitivity. Reference 1 cites Marshall & Burgoyne (1976, same journal), indicating a companion paper.

What It Contributed To

  • Understanding of chromatin higher-order packing as a distinct structural level above the nucleosome
  • Size-exclusion model for inter-nucleosome gaps in the superstructure
  • Chromatin maturation kinetics after DNA replication (~10 min to reach full protection)
  • Later Burgoyne chromatin superstructure studies (1981–1987 alternating nucleosome series)
  • Broader field of DNase I hypersensitivity as a probe of chromatin structure and gene regulation
  • Burgoyne & Bhakoo (1999) — pyknosis work connecting chromatin condensation to apoptotic cell death, extending the understanding of higher-order chromatin transitions
