Chromatin Superstructure: The Next Level of Structure Above the Nucleosome Has an Alternating Character. A Two-Nucleosome Based Series Is Generated by Probes Armed with DNAase-I Acting on Isolated Nuclei

Authors: L. A. Burgoyne and J. D. Skinner

Journal: Biochemical and Biophysical Research Communications, 99(3), 893–899

Year: 1981

Received: 9 February 1981

DOI: —

Citations: ~30–60 (estimated)

Context & Background

By 1981, three major levels of chromatin coiling had been identified chemically: the 10 bp repeat of DNA on the nucleosome surface, the ~200 bp nucleosome repeat, and massive domains of hundreds of nucleosomes. The level of organisation between the individual nucleosome and these large domains remained poorly understood. Burgoyne's 1976 work had shown that chromatin superstructure protects against DNase I but not micrococcal nuclease, suggesting a size-exclusion mechanism with uniform inter-nucleosome gaps. This paper takes an ingenious approach: attaching DNase I to large protein carriers (ferritin, BSA) via glutaraldehyde crosslinking to create “probes” that are too large to penetrate between closely packed nucleosomes. By analysing the resulting single-strand DNA fragments on alkaline agarose gels, the probe reveals which nucleosomes are physically accessible and which are shielded by their neighbours.

Key Findings

  • Two-nucleosome (2N) based repeat discovered: DNase-I-armed probes attacking intact nuclei generated a fragment series based on ~400 bp (2N) rather than the standard ~200 bp (1N) repeat. This indicates that every second nucleosome in a series is resistant to attack by large probes, while the intervening nucleosomes are accessible.
  • Observed in multiple species: The 2N-based repeat was seen in both chicken erythrocyte nuclei and rat liver nuclei, indicating a conserved feature of chromatin higher-order structure.
  • Multiple probe types give same result: Ferritin-DNase I probes, BSA-DNase I probes, and (weakly) free DNase I alone all produced the 2N-based series, confirming the result is not an artefact of a particular probe.
  • Series extends to at least 8N: Higher-order bands at 4N, 6N, and 8N were observed, indicating that some stretches of the alternating pattern extend for at least 8 nucleosomes. The higher bands (6N, 8N) were more diffuse and sometimes required multiple gel tracks to confirm.
  • Four structural models proposed: Fig. 5 illustrates four nucleosome folding patterns that could generate the alternating accessibility: (a) zigzag nucleofilament with alternating projection, (b) sinusoidal nucleofilament, (c) compact helical fold, (d) coil-stacking pattern. Models a–c generate the alternation at the nucleofilament level; model d generates it by coil geometry. All are compatible with the Thoma, Koller & Klug (1979) EM zigzag structure.
  • Ca-Mg endonuclease suppression method: Developed a novel, reversible method for suppressing the intrinsic Ca-Mg endonuclease of rat liver nuclei using sodium dithiobisnitrobenzoate (DTNB). Inhibition was not complete but sufficient for these studies and was fully reversible with thiol agents (2-mercaptoethanol). Not needed for chicken erythrocytes (which lack the enzyme).
  • Asymmetric 2N peaks: After extended digestion, many 2N peaks were clearly asymmetric, suggesting the susceptible zones on accessible nucleosomes contain multiple cleavage sites rather than a single site.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper establishes the dinucleosome as the basic repeating unit of chromatin higher-order structure — a level of organisation between the individual nucleosome and the large chromatin domains. The concept that every second nucleosome is physically shielded by its neighbours while the intervening ones project outward is a direct structural consequence of the zigzag folding model, and Burgoyne was among the first to demonstrate this biochemically. The probe engineering approach (attaching DNase I to large carriers) was creative and ahead of its time, essentially using molecular size as a variable to probe structural accessibility.

Career significance: This paper marks the beginning of Burgoyne's long and productive collaboration with J. D. Skinner. It also represents the lab's first US funding (Department of Health, Education and Welfare), suggesting growing international recognition. The paper cites Burgoyne & Skinner (1979) J. Cell Sci. as reference 8, confirming the collaboration began at least two years earlier. The alternating nucleosome concept became a central theme of Burgoyne's chromatin research through the 1980s, with several subsequent papers refining and extending this finding.

What It Contributed To

  • Establishment of the dinucleosome as the structural unit of chromatin higher-order packing
  • Biochemical evidence for the zigzag model of nucleosome folding
  • Novel probe engineering approach (DNase I on protein carriers) for probing chromatin accessibility
  • Reversible DTNB method for Ca-Mg endonuclease suppression in rat liver nuclei
  • Subsequent Burgoyne papers on dinucleosomal repeats (1982 avian erythrocyte, 1983 trypsin resistance, 1987 coherent series)
  • Broader understanding of chromatin fibre geometry and nucleosome packing
  • Later work connecting chromatin condensation to cell death mechanisms (1999 pyknosis paper) and continuing research

Key Collaborators
