Unstable, Heterogeneous Physical States of Nuclear Bodies During the Post-Hepatectomy Dedifferentiation–Replication–Redifferentiation Sequence

Authors: A. J. Marshall and L. A. Burgoyne

Journal: Cell Differentiation, 6, 263–272

Year: 1977

Accepted: 18 June 1977

DOI: —

Citations: ~15–30 (estimated)

Nuclear Density Liver Regeneration Hepatectomy Nuclear Purification Dedifferentiation Cell Cycle Methodology Sucrose Gradient

Context & Background

This paper, the third collaboration between A. J. Marshall and Burgoyne, extends their methodological concerns about nuclear preparation procedures (Marshall & Burgoyne, 1976) into the domain of cell differentiation and the cell cycle. Using the well-characterised model of rat liver regeneration after partial hepatectomy, they track changes in nuclear buoyant density on sucrose gradients over an 8-day regenerative period. The key question is whether nuclei undergo generalised physical changes of state during the dedifferentiation–replication–redifferentiation sequence, and whether standard purification methods inadvertently select against certain nuclear states.

Key Findings

  • Cyclic changes in nuclear density during regeneration: Unwashed nuclei isolated directly from liver homogenates showed a cyclic density shift — decreasing from ~2.4 M to ~2.3 M sucrose equilibrium density during the first 2–3 days post-hepatectomy (coinciding with S-phase and mitosis), then increasing back to ~2.45 M sucrose by day 8 as redifferentiation proceeds.
  • Massive density heterogeneity at day 3: At 3 days post-hepatectomy, the nuclear population showed extreme density heterogeneity with no clear peak — consistent with an asynchronous population of cells in various states of the dedifferentiation–replication sequence.
  • Effect lost in washed nuclei: These density changes were not observable in extensively purified (washed, polyamine-stabilised) nuclei, which showed a constant density of ~2.5 M sucrose throughout the regenerative period. This means the density-altering components are stripped away during purification.
  • True density equilibrium, not size effect: Nuclei loaded at the gradient bottom rose to the same position as those loaded at the top, confirming the distribution reflects genuine buoyant density, not sedimentation rate or size.
  • Molecular redistribution between nuclei and supernatant: Mixing experiments using dual-label (3H/14C thymidine) washed and unwashed nuclei demonstrated that nuclei in fresh homogenates both lose and gain macromolecules. Washed nuclei exposed to unwashed nuclei in homogenate shifted slightly in density, indicating bidirectional molecular interchange.
  • Net density shifts reflect protein/lipid loss: Depleted (washed) nuclei were denser, implying the lost material has average density lower than nuclei — consistent with protein and/or lipid loss. RNA loss would lower density, so RNA is not the explanation.
  • Selective loss warning: Standard nuclear purification through 2.1–2.4 M sucrose cushions would selectively exclude the lower-density nuclear types present during regeneration, creating a non-random, biased sample of the original nuclear population. Nuclei from dividing or recently-divided cells would be preferentially lost.
  • Possible link to nuclear protein matrix: Since the density cycle begins after S-phase and during/after mitosis, it may reflect re-establishment of the nuclear protein matrix (Berezney et al.) or accumulation of structural proteins during redifferentiation.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper highlights a fundamental and underappreciated problem in nuclear biology: standard purification procedures are inherently selective, and this selectivity varies with the developmental and metabolic state of the tissue. The demonstration that nuclei undergo a cyclic change in physical state during the hepatectomy regeneration sequence, and that this change is invisible after standard purification, is an important cautionary finding. The molecular redistribution experiments further complicate interpretation by showing that nuclei in homogenates are not static objects but dynamically exchange macromolecules with their environment.

Career significance: This is the third Marshall & Burgoyne paper and represents a broadening of their methodological programme from chromatin biochemistry into cell biology and differentiation. The paper links nuclear structure to the cell cycle and differentiation state, themes that would become important in Burgoyne's later work on chromatin condensation, pyknosis, and cell death. Published in Cell Differentiation rather than a nucleic acids journal, it shows the Burgoyne lab engaging with a wider audience. Marshall continues as first author.

What It Contributed To

  • Awareness that nuclear purification procedures select non-randomly against certain developmental states
  • Understanding that nuclear physical state changes cyclically with the cell cycle / differentiation state
  • Evidence that nuclei dynamically exchange macromolecules with cytoplasmic supernatant during preparation
  • Foundation for later work connecting chromatin condensation states to cell cycle and differentiation
  • Broader theme of the Burgoyne lab: rigorous methodology and awareness of preparation artefacts
