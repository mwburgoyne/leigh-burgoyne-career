Arbitrarily Primed PCR for Comparison of Meta Genomes and Extracting Useful Loci from Them

Authors: Leigh Burgoyne, Lin Y. Koh, David Catcheside

Journal: Methods in Molecular Biology, Vol. 1620 (Chapter 18), pp. 267–280

Year: 2017

DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4939-7060-5_18

Citations: ~5 (OpenAlex)

Context & Background

By 2017, Burgoyne’s soil metagenomics programme had progressed through a decade of development: from the original SMIPS arbitrary primer patent (2004), through microarray-based sequence content profiling (Waters et al. 2012), to high throughput sequencing validation (Khodakova et al. 2013, 2014), and the 50mer condensation concept introduced at the ISFG Congress (Burgoyne, Koh & Catcheside 2015). This chapter in the prestigious Methods in Molecular Biology series — part of the volume PCR: Methods and Protocols edited by Lucília Domingues — represents the definitive, peer-reviewed methods publication codifying the entire workflow. As corresponding and first author, Burgoyne retained direct intellectual ownership of the programme. This methods chapter codified the arbitrarily primed PCR workflow for the forensic soil programme.

Key Findings

  • Complete AP-PCR protocol: A single 18mer primer (“antiseq05”: GGAGGTGGTGTTCGAGGG) is used with two low-stringency acquisition cycles followed by 35 high-stringency cycles, exploiting inherent PCR amplification bias to generate a reproducible, self-selected subset of sequences from any metagenome.
  • 50mer sequence tag extraction: Discriminating 50mer oligonucleotides are computationally mined from amplicon reads using a sum-of-squares deviation (SSQD) filter on dinucleotide frequencies, selecting only those approximating random sequence composition. Each read contributes at most one 50mer, avoiding bias toward long amplimers.
  • Scattergram-based comparison: Pairwise metagenome comparisons are visualised as 2D scattergrams of 50mer frequencies. Unrelated soils share no 50mers (all points on axes); related soils show off-axis shared points. As few as 3,000 reads of ~250 bases can suffice for discrimination.
  • Sequence-coding multiplexing: Eight 26mer tagged primers are provided for multiplexed massively parallel sequencing of multiple samples in a single run, with built-in tolerance for terminal base loss.
  • Locus-specific primer extraction: Demonstrated extraction of a targeted primer pair (SDNf1/SDNr1) from a high-frequency 50mer in suburban soil, yielding a 262 bp product with 77% homology to Rubrobacter radiotolerans (a Portuguese hot spring extremophile). Testing on 15 South Australian soils showed presence in 8, all from semi-arid or unwatered locations — illustrating the pathway from metagenome profiling to region-specific diagnostic markers.
  • Compact data architecture: The entire method produces small, spreadsheet-manageable datasets suitable for building in-house forensic databases, in contrast to the terabyte-scale outputs of conventional shotgun metagenomics.

Significance & Contribution

This chapter represents the definitive methodological codification of Burgoyne’s arbitrarily primed PCR metagenome comparison system — the culmination of a research programme stretching from the 2004 SMIPS patent through a decade of iterative development. Published in Methods in Molecular Biology, the leading protocols series in molecular biology, it provides the first complete, step-by-step, reproducible description of the workflow from soil collection through DNA extraction, two-round PCR amplification, massively parallel sequencing, computational 50mer extraction, and scattergram-based comparison. The chapter goes beyond the 2015 conference paper by adding multiplexing primers, detailed data processing algorithms, and the Rubrobacter locus-specific primer extraction example — demonstrating the full analytical pipeline from environmental sample to actionable forensic marker. This chapter stands as the definitive methodological reference for a programme that began with yeast enzyme crystallography in 1966, encompassed the co-discovery of chromatin substructure in 1973, and the invention of FTA paper technology in the 1990s, before branching into forensic soil metagenomics and, most recently, the 2025 macrofungi DNA extraction work with Catcheside.

What It Contributed To

  • Provides the definitive, citable methods reference for any laboratory seeking to implement AP-PCR metagenome comparison
  • Established a complete pipeline from soil collection to forensic marker design, closing the gap between research demonstration and operational forensic use
  • Introduced multiplexing primers enabling cost-effective parallel processing of multiple soil samples
  • Demonstrated extraction of locus-specific diagnostic primers from metagenome data, exemplified by the Rubrobacter-related arid soil marker
  • Created a framework for building regional forensic soil databases with modest computing and storage requirements
