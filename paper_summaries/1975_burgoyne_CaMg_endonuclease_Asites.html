The Reaction of the Ca-Mg Endonuclease with the A-Sites of Rat Nucleoprotein

Authors: L. A. Burgoyne and J. Mobbs

Journal: Nucleic Acids Research, 2(9), 1551–1558

Year: 1975

Received: 7 July 1975

DOI: —

Citations: ~30–60 (estimated)

Chromatin A-Sites Ca-Mg Endonuclease Nucleosome Micrococcal Nuclease Trypsin Quantitative

Context & Background

By 1975, the Hewish & Burgoyne chromatin substructure discovery (1973) had been confirmed and extended by Kornberg, Noll, Olins & Olins, and others. The nucleosome model was established and micrococcal nuclease had become the standard tool for chromatin digestion. This paper returns to the Ca-Mg endonuclease — the endogenous nuclear enzyme that originally revealed the substructure — and provides a rigorous quantitative characterisation of how it attacks the regularly spaced susceptible sites, which Burgoyne now names the "A-sites." This is the first paper without Hewish as co-author on the chromatin programme; Jennifer Mobbs is promoted to full co-author.

Key Findings

  • Quantitative characterisation of A-site cleavage: In a standard 30-min digest, 50–60% of potential single-stranded A-sites were cleaved, only 0.5–1.5% of DNA was rendered acid-soluble, and only 1.5–4% of chromatin became buffer-soluble. The enzyme is highly specific — it cleaves extensively at A-sites while generating minimal degradation products.
  • Cleavage is evenly distributed throughout the genome: P values (probability of cleavage at any given A-site) estimated independently from band 1 and from the zone of bands slower than band 2 agreed closely (Figure 2). This demonstrates no gross clustering of endonuclease-susceptible sites — the A-sites are attacked uniformly across the bulk genome.
  • Single-strand breaks are independent: Pds (double-strand cleavage probability) approximately equalled (Pss)2, indicating that single-strand breaks on opposite strands at the same A-site occur independently of each other. No positive or negative cooperativity.
  • Low-frequency cleavages occur between A-sites: A class of very fast-migrating fragments appeared, especially prominent in denatured DNA. These arise from rare endonucleolytic cleavages in the protected regions between A-sites.
  • Histones are stable during digestion: No significant loss of any histone class over 30 minutes. F1, F2a1, and (F3+F2b+F2a2) ratios remained constant through 60 minutes — the enzyme acts on DNA without disrupting the histone complement.
  • Trypsin-resistant core retains A-site specificity: Digesting nuclei to a trypsin-resistant core (removing histone tails and non-histone proteins) inhibited Ca-Mg endonuclease activity but did not alter the specificity — the standard DNA fragment pattern persisted. Trypsin did not expose new sites or broaden bands. The A-site steric protection is intrinsic to the core histone-DNA complex.
  • Micrococcal nuclease attacks the same A-sites: Mixed digests using both Ca-Mg endonuclease and micrococcal nuclease together produced fragment patterns identical to single-enzyme digests. If they attacked different sites with the same spacing, complex mixed patterns would result. This confirms both enzymes recognise the same accessible regions of chromatin.
  • Mathematical model for random cleavage: Derived the relationship: expected proportion of DNA in band N = N(1−P)N−1P2, which fits the observed data well and provides a theoretical framework for analysing nuclease digestion kinetics.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper provided the first rigorous quantitative framework for understanding nuclease digestion of chromatin at the nucleosome level. The demonstration that A-site cleavage is uniform across the genome, that single-strand breaks are statistically independent, and that micrococcal nuclease attacks the same sites as the endogenous Ca-Mg endonuclease were all important for the maturing nucleosome field. The trypsin experiment was particularly elegant — showing that the core histone-DNA structure alone defines A-site accessibility.

Career significance: This is the first chromatin paper without Hewish. Burgoyne is now leading the programme independently with Mobbs. The naming of "A-sites" represents Burgoyne's effort to establish precise terminology and anticipates the complexity of chromatin accessibility that would later become a major research theme. The mathematical modelling shows Burgoyne's quantitative rigour. The paper explicitly engages with Noll's and Kornberg's work, placing Burgoyne's contribution in context with the now-established nucleosome field.

  • Confirmation that micrococcal nuclease and endogenous Ca-Mg endonuclease attack the same linker DNA sites
  • Understanding that core histone-DNA structure defines nuclease accessibility (A-sites)
  • Later Burgoyne chromatin higher-order structure studies (1976–1987)
  • Established "A-site" terminology for the inter-nucleosomal linker region
