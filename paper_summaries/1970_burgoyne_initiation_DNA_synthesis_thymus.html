Initiation of DNA Synthesis in Rat Thymus: Correlation of Calcium-Dependent Initiation in Thymocytes and in Isolated Thymus Nuclei

Authors: Leigh A. Burgoyne, M. Anwar Waqar, Maurice R. Atkinson

Journal: Biochemical and Biophysical Research Communications, 39(5), 918–922

Year: 1970

DOI: 10.1016/0006-291X(70)90418-5

Citations: ~30–50 (connects in vitro nuclear system to known in vivo physiology)

DNA Replication Calcium Signalling Thymus Cell Division Control Cyclic AMP

Context & Background

This companion paper to Burgoyne, Waqar & Atkinson (1970, BBRC 39:254) extends the Ca2+-dependent DNA synthesis findings from liver nuclei to thymus nuclei. The thymus was chosen strategically: Whitfield and colleagues had already shown that raising blood Ca2+ levels stimulates mitotic activity in rat thymus, and that 1–2 mM external Ca2+ promotes initiation of DNA synthesis in isolated thymocytes. This paper asks whether the Ca2+-dependent endonuclease mechanism discovered in liver nuclei could explain the known physiological effects of calcium on cell division.

Key Findings

  • Ca2+ effect confirmed in thymus: Thymus nuclei isolated in Ca2+-free solutions (with EDTA/EGTA) had active DNA polymerase (shown by poly d(A-T) incorporation) but near-zero endogenous DNA synthesis. Addition of 1 mM CaCl2 produced rapid, sustained DNA synthesis without added primer — replicating the liver nuclei finding.
  • Ca2+ dose-response characterised: Maximal synthesis at 2 mM CaCl2, half-maximal at ~1 mM, with significant stimulation even at 0.1 mM (despite 1 mM EDTA and 0.2 mM EGTA in the buffer). Apparent free Ca2+ activity of ~30 μM at half-maximal activation, measured with a Ca2+ electrode.
  • DNA nicking confirmed: Alkaline sucrose gradient analysis showed Ca2+ promoted nicking of thymus nuclear DNA, with labelled DNA (8S–13S) smaller than bulk DNA — consistent with synthesis initiating at newly created nicks.
  • Cyclic AMP does not replace Ca2+: Over a wide concentration range (10−9 to 10−4 M), cyclic AMP had no significant effect on DNA synthesis in either thymus or liver nuclei, ruling out the hypothesis (MacManus & Whitfield, 1969) that Ca2+ acts via cAMP.
  • Physiological implications discussed: The correlation between Ca2+-dependent initiation in isolated nuclei and Ca2+-dependent promotion of mitosis in thymocytes in vivo raises the possibility of a physiological role for Ca2+-dependent endonuclease. However, the authors note insightfully that this mechanism alone "is difficult to envisage... accounting for the specificity required for sequential initiation in eukaryotic replicons."

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper bridges the gap between an in vitro nuclear observation and established in vivo physiology. By showing that the Ca2+-dependent endonuclease operates in thymus nuclei — where Ca2+ is known to promote cell division — it strengthened the case for biological relevance. The careful ruling out of cyclic AMP as a mediator was important given the then-fashionable tendency to attribute many cellular effects to cAMP signalling. The final paragraph, noting that Ca2+-dependent nicking cannot provide the specificity needed for replicon initiation, shows prescient scientific judgment — the Ca2+-dependent endonuclease would indeed turn out to be more important for chromatin structure than for replication control.

Career significance: Together with the companion liver paper, this establishes the Burgoyne–Waqar–Atkinson team and the Ca2+-dependent endonuclease research programme at Flinders. The connection to Whitfield's thymocyte work placed their findings in a broader physiological context.
