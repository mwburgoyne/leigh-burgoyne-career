A Rapid Wire-Based Sampling Method for DNA Profiling

Authors: Tong Chen, David E.A. Catcheside, Alice Stephenson, Chris Hefford, K. Paul Kirkbride, and Leigh A. Burgoyne

Journal: Journal of Forensic Sciences, Vol. 57, No. 2, pp. 472–477

Year: 2012

DOI: 10.1111/j.1556-4029.2011.02006.x

Citations: ~2 (OpenAlex)

Type: Technical Note

Context & Background

Disaster victim identification (DVI) often involves the challenging logistics of sorting many tissue fragments from a small number of individuals. Existing workflows for DNA profiling require labor-intensive steps — tissue solubilization, DNA purification, yield estimation, and careful pipetting — all of which demand laboratory infrastructure and considerable time. This paper, funded by the Australian Government through the “Closed-Set Human Identification” project managed by ANZPAA NIFS, addressed the gap by developing a radically simplified sampling method that could bypass DNA extraction entirely and operate on a portable Agilent 2100 Bioanalyser platform. Burgoyne’s group at Flinders University, in collaboration with Forensic Science South Australia and the Australian Federal Police, set out to create a system suitable for remote or field conditions.

Key Findings

  • Hot wire sampling works: Stabbing human tissue or blood clots with stainless steel wire heated to red heat, followed by isopropanol fixation and drying, yielded sufficient DNA for direct PCR amplification — completely bypassing DNA extraction and quantification.
  • Hot wire outperforms cold wire: Hot wire bound more DNA than cold wire, likely because heat-denatured proteins form a gel that promotes tissue adherence. Hot wire also ensured self-sterilization at the moment of sampling.
  • Works across diverse tissues: Full quintuplex STR profiles were obtained from blood clots, prostate, bladder, skin, colon, muscle, liver, lung, kidney, and pancreas. DNA yields ranged from ~0.25 ng (muscle) to ~10.27 ng (kidney) per 2-mm wire tip.
  • Minimal PCR inhibition: A 2-mm wire tip in a 25-μL PCR reduced amplification yield by approximately 25% — an acceptable trade-off for the massive gain in speed and simplicity.
  • Samples stable for months: Wire-mounted samples stored at room temperature remained amplifiable for at least 180 days, with no time-dependent degradation.
  • Compatible with full forensic profiling: Samples on wire could be amplified with AmpFlSTR Profiler Plus (9 loci) at 32 cycles, yielding useful profiles on an AB 3130xl Genetic Analyzer — enabling both rapid field triage and full laboratory genotyping from the same sample.

Significance & Contribution

This paper represents a creative “lateral thinking” approach to forensic DNA technology that is characteristic of Burgoyne’s career. Rather than pursuing ever-more-complex microfluidic integration, the team asked: what if you could skip DNA extraction altogether? The wire-based method is strikingly simple — a heated wire, isopropanol fixation, and direct PCR — yet it solves a real operational problem in disaster victim identification. The dual-mode capability (field triage on a Bioanalyser plus later laboratory analysis from the same wire) is particularly elegant, as it preserves evidentiary value while enabling rapid on-site decisions. The work represents a conceptual descendant of Burgoyne’s FTA paper invention, applying the same “simplify the sample collection” philosophy to hard tissues under austere field conditions.

What It Contributed To

  • Proof of concept for extraction-free, field-deployable DNA profiling in disaster victim identification scenarios
  • Demonstrated feasibility of magnetically manipulable wire substrates for future automated profiling chips
  • Advanced the concept of “sample-on-substrate” approaches where collection medium doubles as PCR-compatible carrier
  • Contributed to the broader literature on direct PCR from minimally processed samples
