Context & Background

This patent (US 5,985,327) is the sixth in the FTA patent family and a continuation of US 5,807,527, itself a continuation of the foundational FTA patent US 5,496,562. Like US 5,807,527, it shares the identical specification and examples as the foundational patent — no new matter was added. The critical distinction lies in its claim strategy. Where the parent continuation (US 5,807,527) used broad functional language covering “protein denaturing agents” and “free radical traps” generally, this patent takes a different tack. Its Claim 1 focuses on a narrower but strategically distinct combination: a solid matrix (filter paper or plastic) with a composition at alkaline pH that includes a free radical trap. Notably, this independent claim omits the protein denaturing agent and chelating agent that were mandatory elements in most prior FTA claims, isolating the alkaline pH plus free radical trap combination as independently protectable. The patent also introduces a new category of method claims (Claims 12–17) specifically directed to plasmid storage — a distinct application not separately claimed in the parent patents. This is the second patent in the family filed on November 26, 1997 (sharing a filing date with US 5,976,572), representing a coordinated filing strategy to extract maximum claim scope from the original specification.

Key Claims & Innovations

  • Claim 1 — Minimal two-element composition: A solid medium for storing genetic material comprising (i) a solid matrix of filter paper or plastic material, and (ii) an effective amount of a composition at alkaline pH including a free radical trap. This strips the claim to its most minimal form — omitting the protein denaturing agent (SDS) and chelating agent (EDTA) that were mandatory in both the foundational patent and the US 5,807,527 continuation. This captures any DNA storage medium using alkaline pH and free radical protection, regardless of whether it also includes detergents or chelators.
  • Claims 2–6 — pH and free radical trap specifics: Alkaline pH provided by monovalent base (Claim 2); specifically by Tris (Claim 3); Tris in free base form (Claim 4); free radical trap is 40 mM uric acid with alkaline pH from 100 mM Tris (Claim 5); free radical trap comprises uric acid (Claim 6). These dependent claims progressively narrow to the specific preferred embodiment.
  • Claim 7 — Optional addition of denaturing and chelating agents: Composition further comprises a protein denaturing agent and chelating agent. This positions the full FTA formulation (SDS + EDTA) as a dependent claim, broadening the scope of the independent claim.
  • Claims 8–11 — Further dependent specifics: Protein denaturing agent is anionic detergent (Claim 8); specifically SDS (Claim 9); chelating agent is EDTA (Claim 10); matrix is cellulose-based paper (Claim 11). These narrow to the exact FTA formulation.
  • Claims 12–17 — Plasmid storage method: A method specifically for storing a plasmid on the solid medium, with parallel dependent claims for base, Tris, uric acid, and cellulose matrix. This is a strategic addition — explicitly claiming plasmid storage as a distinct application of the FTA technology, not separately covered by earlier method claims focused on blood DNA or general genetic material.

Significance & Contribution

Patent strategy contribution: This continuation represents a sophisticated claim-splitting approach. By stripping the independent claim down to its two most fundamental elements — alkaline pH and a free radical trap on a solid matrix — Burgoyne carved out a distinct claim scope that does not require a protein denaturing agent or chelating agent. This is strategically important because competitors might develop DNA storage media that use alkaline buffering and antioxidant protection without anionic detergents, and these would still fall within the scope of Claim 1. The protein denaturing agent and chelating agent appear only as dependent Claim 7, making them optional enhancements rather than mandatory elements. The plasmid storage method claims (12–17) open a separate application domain — the specification describes long-term archival storage of cloned DNA on record cards with polystyrene encasement, and these method claims specifically protect that use case.

Career significance: This is the sixth US patent in the FTA family and the fourth granted within calendar years 1998–1999, demonstrating the intensity of Flinders Technologies’ patent prosecution campaign during this period. Filed on the same day as US 5,976,572 (which covered the analytical platform branch), this patent covers the storage-only branch with minimal claim elements. Together the two filings represent a coordinated strategy to cover the FTA invention from both the simple storage angle and the analytical platform angle. The terminal disclaimer confirms all these continuations share a common term, but each provides distinct claim scope for enforcement and licensing purposes. The Seaford address confirms Burgoyne’s consistent South Australian location throughout the patent family.
