The Interspersed DNA Repeat Conserved in Water Birds’ Genomes

Authors: X. Zhu, L.A. Burgoyne and J.D. Skinner

Journal: Genome, 34, 493–494

Year: 1991

Institution: School of Biology, Flinders University of South Australia

Corresponding Editor: D.L. Baillie

Received: 25 February 1991; Accepted 26 February 1991

DOI: —

Citations: ~10–20 (estimated)

EMBL Accession: X57379

Interspersed Repeat Water Birds Anseriformes Avian Genome TaqI 425 bp Repeat Mallard Muscovy Duck Goose Black Swan

Context & Background

Following the 1990 characterisation of tandem repeats in the Toulouse goose, this short note extends the avian repetitive DNA programme to a different class of repeat — interspersed elements — and to a broader taxonomic range spanning four genera of the order Anseriformes. The paper was accepted the day after submission, reflecting its concise, definitive nature.

Key Findings

  • 425 bp interspersed repeat: A major interspersed repeat family was identified by TaqI restriction, M13 cloning, and sequencing from four water bird species: Mallard (Anas platyrhynchos), Muscovy Duck (Cairina moschata), Toulouse Goose (Anser anser), and Black Swan (Cygnus atratus).
  • Conserved across four genera: The repeat is conserved across at least four genera of the order Anseriformes: Anas, Anser, Cairina, and Cygnus. A 425 bp consensus core sequence was derived and deposited in EMBL (accession X57379).
  • Ancient origin: Individual isolates show only 80–90% homology within an individual or between genera, suggesting the sequence is very old with an origin that predates the separation of these genera.
  • Genome proportion: The full-length parental sequence may be ~2.170 kb (largest observed TaqI band). The 425 bp core unit represents approximately 2% of the avian genome; the full repeat is about 10% of the genome.
  • No internal repetition: Unlike the tandem repeats characterised in the 1990 paper, this repeat shows no evidence of internal tandem structure.
  • Weak chicken homology: The consensus sequence shows a weak but probably significant relationship (65% identity) to the published chicken CR1 OVA sequence (Stumph et al. 1981), suggesting a possible connection to the broader chicken repeat 1 (CR1) family of avian interspersed elements.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper identified a major interspersed repeat element conserved across the order Anseriformes, representing one of the earliest characterisations of interspersed (non-tandem) repetitive DNA in water birds. The finding that this element constitutes ~10% of the genome and predates genus separation provided insight into the deep evolutionary architecture of avian genomes. The weak homology to chicken CR1 OVA hinted at a relationship to the CR1 non-LTR retrotransposon family, which was later confirmed to be a major component of avian genomes.

Career significance: Second Zhu/Burgoyne/Skinner avian genomics paper, continuing to build the comparative repetitive DNA programme. Note that Burgoyne is now second author (between Zhu and Skinner), reflecting his role as senior/supervising investigator. The department name has changed from “School of Biological Sciences” to “School of Biology.” Sequence deposited in EMBL database — the lab’s growing contribution to public sequence repositories.
