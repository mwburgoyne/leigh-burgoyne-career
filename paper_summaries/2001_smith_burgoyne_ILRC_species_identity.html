Species Identity: Conserved Inverted LINE Repeat Clusters (ILRC) in the Vertebrate Genome as Indicators of Population Boundaries

Authors: L.M. Smith and L.A. Burgoyne

Journal: Gene, 271, 273–283

Year: 2001

PMID: 11418249

Institution: School of Biological Sciences, Flinders University of South Australia

Citations: ~4 (OpenAlex)

Context & Background

Building on the laboratory’s decade of experience with avian repetitive DNA (Zhu, Skinner & Burgoyne, 1990–1992), this paper asked a new question: when LINE retroelements insert into each other to form inverted repeat clusters, do these structures behave as species-specific markers? The work also demonstrated practical use of FTA paper for DNA sample storage from 16 avian species and multiple mammals.

Key Findings

  • ILRCs form by sequential super-insertion: Avian CR1 LINE elements insert into one another to form inverted clusters with no host DNA between elements. Multiple CR1 subfamilies (48–82% homology) confirmed sequential retrotransposition events.
  • Species-characteristic patterns: Single-primer PCR across 14 avian species produced species-specific band patterns. Within species (20 Crested Pigeons, 20 Pelicans), ILRC sizes were conserved; between unrelated species, no shared sizes were found.
  • Closely related species share ILRCs: White-Winged and Superb Wrens shared most ILRC loci, with only one band unique to one species. Chicken breeds showed breed-specific bands alongside shared ones.
  • ILRCs decay by point mutation: Sequencing of a 183 bp ILRC from seven Crested Pigeons revealed accumulating point mutations. Present in 14 of 16 birds.
  • Mammalian L1 also forms ILRCs: Conserved species-specific ILRC patterns demonstrated in humans, rats, horse, cow, and cat.
  • Positive selection implied: High ratio of conserved to variant ILRCs across both bottlenecked and large populations argued against neutral drift alone.

Significance & Contribution

Identified a previously unrecognised class of conserved genomic structures — inverted LINE repeat clusters — that form by retrotranspositional super-insertion and spread rapidly to fixation within species. Proposed as a universal vertebrate species identification tool requiring only a single degenerate primer per LINE family. Connects Burgoyne’s earlier avian repetitive DNA work with practical forensic and conservation applications. All samples processed using FTA paper.

  • FTA paper validated for wildlife and museum specimen DNA across diverse taxa
  • Proposed functional role for LINE inverted repeats in gene regulation
  • Extended species-specific repetitive DNA concept from satellites to retrotransposon clusters
  • Twelve ILRC sequences deposited in EMBL database
