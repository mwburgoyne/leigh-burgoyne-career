Bacterial Typing: Storing and Processing of Stabilized Reference Bacteria for Polymerase Chain Reaction without Preparing DNA — An Example of an Automatable Procedure

Authors: Craig Rogers and Leigh A. Burgoyne

Journal: Analytical Biochemistry, 247, 223–227

Year: 1997

Received: August 15, 1996

Institution: School of Biological Sciences, Flinders University, Bedford Park, South Australia 5042

Phase: Phase 4 — DNA Forensics, FTA Technology & Applied Biology

FTA Technology Bacterial Typing PCR Ribotyping Staphylococcus aureus DNA Storage Automation Microbial Forensics 16S-23S Intergenic Spacer

Context & Background

By 1997, FTA card technology had been well established for the storage and processing of human blood DNA, validated by Belgrader, Marino, and Williams (1995) and underpinned by Burgoyne's foundational solid medium patent (US 5,496,562). This paper represents the first peer-reviewed journal publication extending FTA technology from eukaryotic (blood) samples to prokaryotic (bacterial) applications. Rogers and Burgoyne demonstrated that bacteria applied to FTA paper are killed on contact, creating “DNA-containing ghosts” with cell walls rendered passive and completely penetrable to PCR reagents. The test application was PCR ribotyping — amplification of the 16S–23S rRNA intergenic spacer region, following the method of Kostman et al. (1995) — using five Staphylococcus strains and one E. coli strain. The work was explicitly designed with automation compatibility in mind, reflecting the commercialisation trajectory established in the 1996 apparatus patent (WO 96/11406).

Key Findings

  • FTA kills bacteria on contact: Cell walls are rendered “passive and completely penetrable” to PCR reagents, eliminating any need for lysozyme or enzymatic pre-treatment. The bacteria become DNA-containing ghosts that serve directly as PCR templates.
  • No DNA extraction required: FTA retains DNA in situ on the matrix rather than solubilising it — a key advantage over boiling, chaotropic, and resin-based methods. Bacterial DNA on FTA paper can be used directly as a PCR template without purification, estimation, or two-phase extraction.
  • Equivalent wash protocols: Two wash protocols were tested — a stringent phenolic wash and a simple water wash — both gave equivalent ribotype patterns for S. aureus, demonstrating that the simpler protocol is sufficient for routine use.
  • Broad template concentration window: Undiluted to 1/100 dilution of overnight bacterial culture all yielded the same ribotype patterns. Even 1/1000 dilution (~3 μl containing approximately 3 pg DNA) was still detectable.
  • Long-term storage stability: S. aureus stored for 1.6 years at room temperature on FTA paper still produced good amplicon yield, demonstrating the suitability of FTA for long-term bacterial reference collections.
  • Discriminatory power: Four distinct ribotype patterns were obtained from four randomly selected S. aureus clinical isolates, demonstrating significant discriminatory power for strain typing.
  • Automation compatibility: All procedures were designed for automation — only four wash operations required, all simple repetitions, with no two-phase extractions or complex manipulations. This makes the protocol directly compatible with robotic liquid handling systems.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper is the first peer-reviewed demonstration that FTA card technology works with bacteria, not just blood. By showing that FTA paper kills bacteria on contact and renders their DNA directly accessible to PCR without extraction, Rogers and Burgoyne opened an entirely new application domain for FTA technology. The paper effectively extended the FTA platform from eukaryotic DNA storage to microbial applications, laying the groundwork for microbial forensics, high-throughput bacterial strain typing, and automation-compatible bacterial sample archiving. The conceptual link drawn between bacterial autolysis on FTA and eukaryotic cell apoptosis/necrosis (reference 4 in the paper) foreshadowed Burgoyne's later exploration of pyknosis in his 1999 paper.

Career significance: Craig Rogers appears for the first time as lead author, likely representing a student or early-career collaborator working under Burgoyne's supervision at Flinders University. The paper demonstrates the expanding scope of FTA technology from its forensic DNA origins into microbiology and clinical diagnostics. The explicit emphasis on automation compatibility reflects the commercial trajectory of FTA — connecting to the 1996 apparatus patent and the broader vision of high-throughput sample processing that was driving the technology's adoption in forensic DNA databasing programmes worldwide.
