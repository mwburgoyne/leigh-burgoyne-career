Genetic Variation at Three DYS-STR Loci and Amelogenin Between the Australian Caucasians and Three Asian Ethnic Groups

Context & Background

By 2002, Y-chromosome short tandem repeats (Y-STRs) were becoming increasingly important in forensic DNA analysis. Unlike autosomal STRs, Y-STRs are passed exclusively through the paternal lineage without recombination, making them valuable for male lineage identification, sexual assault cases with mixed DNA samples, and population genetics studies. However, the forensic utility of Y-STR markers depends critically on understanding their allele frequency distributions across different ethnic populations, as haplotype frequencies can vary dramatically between groups.

This paper represents a collaboration between the Forensic Science Centre of South Australia and Burgoyne's laboratory at Flinders University. It leverages Burgoyne's FTA paper technology — the study used FTA cards for blood sample collection and DNA extraction, applying the aqueous processing method that had become standard for forensic casework. The study addressed a practical forensic need in Australia's multicultural society: establishing Y-STR reference databases for Australian Caucasians and three major Asian ethnic groups (Malays, Chinese, and Indians), populations frequently encountered in Australian forensic casework.

Key Findings

  • Population samples analysed: 468 unrelated males across four groups — Australian Caucasians (n=130), Malaysian Malays (n=113), Malaysian Chinese (n=113), and Malaysian Indians (n=112). All samples were collected on FTA paper and processed using the aqueous extraction method with 1.2 mm diameter punches.
  • Fluorescent multiplex system developed: Simultaneous amplification of three Y-STR loci (DYS438, DYS390, DYS439) plus amelogenin sex marker using a three-dye system (6-FAM, JOE, TAMRA). This multiplex design enabled efficient population screening with minimal DNA consumption.
  • Locus diversity patterns: Y-STR diversity values ranged from 0.365 to 0.774 across populations. DYS438 showed the greatest inter-population variation, while DYS439 showed the least. Highest diversity was observed for DYS390 in Malays (0.774) and for DYS439 in Indians (0.734).
  • 74 distinct haplotypes identified: Across the four populations, 74 different three-locus Y-STR haplotypes were found. Thirty-seven (50%) were unique to a single population, demonstrating substantial ethnic differentiation. Only 11 haplotypes (15%) were shared across all four groups.
  • Haplotype diversity highest in Malays: The Malaysian Malay population showed the highest haplotype diversity (0.964), followed by Indians (0.955), Caucasians (0.940), and Chinese (0.928), indicating that Southeast Asian Malays may have greater paternal lineage diversity than other studied groups.
  • Strong ethnic marker identified — DYS438-allele 12: The DYS438-allele 12 (232 bp) exists at high frequency (50%) in Australian Caucasians but is nearly absent in all three Asian groups (0.9-1.8%), making it a powerful ethnic ancestry marker. DYS438-allele 13 (237 bp) was found only in Caucasians (3.9%), further reinforcing ethnic differentiation at this locus.
  • Amelogenin null allele discovered: Four male Indian individuals (3.6% of the Indian sample) showed complete absence of the Y-amelogenin allele (112 bp) while retaining the X-amelogenin allele. This amelogenin null mutation, if unrecognised, could lead to false sex determination in forensic casework (males misidentified as females).
  • Mixed male DNA validation: A pooled sample from approximately 500 Australian sperm donors (1984 fertility clinic archive) was analysed to validate the Caucasian reference data. The mixed sample showed allele frequencies closely matching the typed Caucasian population (e.g., DYS438-allele 12 at 52.6% vs 55.4%), confirming the representativeness of the database.

Significance & Contribution

Forensic significance: This paper provided the first comprehensive Y-STR reference database for Australian Caucasians and three major Southeast Asian ethnic groups, filling a critical gap in forensic population genetics for the Asia-Pacific region. The demonstration that DYS438-allele 12 is effectively a Caucasian ethnic marker (50% frequency vs <2% in Asians) provided forensic investigators with a powerful ancestry inference tool. The discovery of amelogenin null alleles in 3.6% of Indians warned forensic laboratories that sex determination based solely on amelogenin could produce false negatives, a finding with direct casework implications.

FTA technology validation: The study provided further validation of FTA paper in large-scale population genetics studies, demonstrating that 1.2 mm punches processed via the aqueous method yield reliable, reproducible results across nearly 500 samples. The successful analysis of a 1984 archived mixed DNA sample (18 years old at time of analysis) reinforced FTA's long-term DNA preservation capabilities, relevant for cold case investigations.

Methodological contribution: The three-locus multiplex system with three fluorescent dyes provided a template for efficient Y-STR population screening. The validation approach using pooled male DNA (sperm donor sample) was innovative and resource-efficient, providing an independent check on population allele frequencies without requiring individual re-typing.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Burgoyne FTA patent series (1993-1999): Entire study depends on FTA paper technology for blood sample collection, DNA immobilisation, and aqueous extraction — the foundation of the workflow
  • Ayub et al. (2000) Nucleic Acids Research: Source of DYS438 marker characterisation used in the multiplex panel
  • Y-STR Haplotype Reference Database (http://ystr.charite.de): International Y-STR standardisation resource used for allele nomenclature
  • DNA Commission of the International Society for Forensic Haemogenetics (1994): STR nomenclature and reporting standards
  • Nei (1987) Molecular Evolutionary Genetics: Statistical framework for calculating locus diversity and haplotype diversity values
  • Applied Biosystems AmpliTaq Gold and ABI PRISM 377 platform: Standard forensic DNA typing infrastructure of the late 1990s/early 2000s

What It Contributed To

  • First large-scale Y-STR reference database for Australian Caucasian and three Southeast Asian ethnic populations
  • Identification of DYS438-allele 12 as a highly discriminating Caucasian ancestry marker (50% vs <2% in Asians)
  • Warning to forensic community about amelogenin null alleles in South Asian populations (3.6% in Indians), with implications for sex determination reliability
  • Validation of FTA paper for large-scale population genetics studies (468 samples processed successfully)
  • Demonstration that 18-year-old archived mixed DNA on FTA paper remains analysable, supporting cold case applications
  • Contributed haplotype data to international Y-STR reference databases (data made publicly available at http://daphne.bio.flinders.edu.au/jc-y_str/)
  • Provided foundational population data for forensic casework interpretation in Australian laboratories dealing with multicultural evidence
