Avian Erythrocyte Chromatin Degradation: The Progressive Exposure of the Dinucleosomal Repeat by Bovine-Pancreatic-DNAase-I-Armed Probes and Free DNAase-I

Authors: Leigh A. Burgoyne and Jennifer D. Skinner

Journal: Nucleic Acids Research, 10(2), 665–673

Year: 1982

Received: 3 December 1981; Accepted 11 December 1981

DOI: —

Citations: ~30–50 (estimated)

Dinucleosomal Repeat DNase I Probes Chicken Erythrocyte Chromatin Superstructure Zigzag Nucleofilament Unilateral Protection Actin-DNase I Limit Digest

Context & Background

This paper is a detailed follow-up to Burgoyne & Skinner (1981 BBRC) which first reported the 2N-based repeat. Using chicken erythrocyte nuclei (chosen for their low contaminating endonuclease activity), this study examines both native and denatured DNA fragments from ferritin-DNase I probes, BSA-DNase I probes, and free DNase I, analysing the progressive time course of digestion and the fine structure of the fragment peaks. The key advance is the discovery that susceptible nucleosomes have two distinct attack sites ~70 bp apart, and that the near-complete absence of mononucleosomal (1N) fragments from bound probe digests implies the existence of another level of structure above the zigzag nucleofilament.

Key Findings

  • 2N series extends to 10–12N: Double-stranded fragments from ferritin-DNase I digestion showed a 2N-based series traceable to at least 10–12N, with no clearly defined end-point. This considerably extends the 8N limit observed in the 1981 BBRC paper.
  • Two attack sites per susceptible nucleosome (~70 bp apart): The 2N size class showed multiple peaks: a main 2N peak and a 2N−70 bp peak (which appeared later in digestion), plus a very weak 2N+70 bp peak. Interpreted as the two turns of DNA around the nucleosome each having one region of maximal exposure to the probe.
  • Bound DNase I produces negligible 1N fragments: A critical contrast with free DNase I, which produces significant 1N-sized single-stranded pieces. With bound probes, the digestion limit species were 2N−70 bp and 2N for both single-stranded and double-stranded fragments.
  • Unilateral protection implies higher-order folding rules: The near-absence of 1N fragments from bound probe digests means the zigzag nucleofilament folds or coils in a way that consistently protects one side from attack. This one-sided protection cannot arise from a random mass of uniformly sticky filaments — it requires definite structural rules for nucleofilament folding. This is taken as evidence for another level of structure above the alternating nucleofilament.
  • Sterically restricted “core” at ~4N: All digestions showed strong deceleration with time, approaching a limit average fragment size of approximately 4N (native). However, this putative “core” is not a single integral multiple of nucleosomes — digests were still stopping while 4N and 6N peaks remained.
  • Double-strand:single-strand break ratio of ~1:1: Indicates a very strong tendency for closely paired or grouped breaks rather than randomly dispersed breaks on opposite strands.
  • Free DNase I concentration-dependent selectivity: Low concentrations of free DNase I were nearly as structurally selective as bound probes. Proposed that free DNase I slowly binds to non-histone proteins such as actin (a common chromatin contaminant), forming actin-DNase I complexes that have both increased effective molecular diameter and intrinsic nuclease activity.
  • Structural signature concept: Articulated the principle that a nucleolytic probe generates fragments reflecting the distances between sites accessible to it — a “structural signature” that is a function of both the chromatin structures present and the molecular diameter of the probe.

Significance & Contribution

Scientific contribution: This paper substantially deepens the characterisation of the dinucleosomal repeat first reported in 1981. The key conceptual advance is the inference that the zigzag nucleofilament must fold according to definite structural rules that produce consistent one-sided protection. This provides biochemical evidence for a level of chromatin organisation between the zigzag nucleofilament and the large chromatin domains — precisely the level that would later be understood as the 30 nm fibre. The “structural signature” concept — that different probe sizes reveal different levels of chromatin organisation — is an elegant framework for interpreting nuclease digestion data. The proposal that free DNase I acquires higher effective molecular diameter by binding to endogenous actin is an insightful explanation for why free DNase I shows some of the same structural selectivity as bound probes.

Career significance: Published in Nucleic Acids Research (a step up from BBRC), this paper establishes the Burgoyne & Skinner dinucleosomal repeat programme as a sustained line of investigation. It also reveals earlier collaborative papers not in the available PDF set: Burgoyne, Skinner & Marshall (1978) J. Cell Sci. 31, 1–11, indicating that Skinner, Marshall and Burgoyne worked together before the collaborations diverged. Continues US funding (Dept. of Health and Welfare).
