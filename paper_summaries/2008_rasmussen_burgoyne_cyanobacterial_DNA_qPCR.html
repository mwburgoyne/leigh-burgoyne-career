Rapid Preparation of Cyanobacterial DNA for Real-Time PCR Analysis

Authors: J.P. Rasmussen, P.H. Barbez, L.A. Burgoyne, and C.P. Saint

Journal: Letters in Applied Microbiology, vol. 46, pp. 14–19

Year: 2008

DOI: 10.1111/j.1472-765X.2007.02252.x

Citations: ~14 (OpenAlex)

Cyanobacteria Real-time PCR DNA Preparation Water Quality Microwave Lysis Field Diagnostics Environmental Monitoring

Context & Background

By the mid-2000s, portable real-time PCR (qPCR) devices were making on-site detection of micro-organisms increasingly feasible for time-critical applications such as monitoring drinking water supplies. A significant bottleneck, however, remained in sample preparation — most DNA extraction methods required fully equipped laboratories, commercial kits, or hazardous reagents such as liquid nitrogen. Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) were of particular concern in Australian freshwaters because certain species of Microcystis, Anabaena, and Cylindrospermopsis can produce dangerous toxins and taste-and-odour compounds. This paper emerged from the Cooperative Research Centre for Water Quality and Treatment, where Burgoyne’s expertise in DNA handling and simplified extraction — honed over decades of forensic and biological work — was applied to an environmental monitoring problem of direct public-health relevance.

Key Findings

  • Three rapid methods compared: Heating during thermocycling, microwave irradiation with a detergent lysis solution, and probe sonication were tested across cultures and field samples of A. circinalis, C. raciborskii, and M. aeruginosa.
  • Heating alone insufficient: Effective only for M. aeruginosa; failed on the more resistant filamentous cyanobacteria due to differences in cell-wall mucilage and sheath structures.
  • Optimised detergent formulation: A novel solution (0.5% Triton X-100, 5 mmol/L DTT, 500 mmol/L Tris–HCl pH 7.5) was developed for microwave lysis, chosen because Triton X-100 is PCR-compatible and avoids inhibition or extra cleanup steps.
  • Microwave matches commercial kit: Microwave irradiation with detergent was statistically equivalent to the commercial Qiagen DNA Mini kit for all three species tested (no significant difference by one-way ANOVA, P ≥ 0.05).
  • Field-portable solution: Sonication was marginally the best overall performer but was impractical for field deployment; microwave lysis was the most field-portable solution, requiring only a benchtop microwave.
  • Broader validation: The method was subsequently validated on additional cylindrospermopsin-producing species including Aphanizomenon ovalisporum and Anabaena bergii.

Significance & Contribution

This paper provided the first validated rapid DNA preparation method applicable to multiple cyanobacterial genera — including the difficult filamentous species — that could be deployed entirely in the field with minimal equipment. By showing that a simple microwave plus detergent protocol could match commercial kit performance, it removed a critical bottleneck in the on-site qPCR monitoring pipeline for toxic algal blooms. The work exemplified Burgoyne’s longstanding philosophy of making DNA-based analyses simpler, cheaper, and more accessible — a thread running from his forensic DNA work through to environmental applications.
